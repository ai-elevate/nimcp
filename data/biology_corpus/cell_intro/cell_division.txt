How a cell becomes two.

Most of the cells in your body are not the cells you were born with. The skin on your hand is replaced about every month. The cells lining your stomach last only a few days. Even your bones, which feel permanent, are constantly being chewed away and rebuilt by specialized cells working in shifts. You are, in a slow and hidden way, a fountain. Old cells fall away. New cells take their place. The shape persists because the rate of replacement matches the rate of loss.

For this fountain to keep flowing, cells must make more cells. They do not do this the way factories make products. A factory takes raw material in one end and ships finished goods out the other. A cell, instead, copies itself. It takes one cell and turns it into two cells, and those two cells are each as alive and complete as the one that came before. This is called cell division.

Picture a cell sitting quietly. It is doing its normal work, making proteins, burning fuel, responding to signals from neighbors. At some point a chemical message arrives, either from outside the cell or from its own internal clock, telling it that more cells are needed in this area. The cell hears the message, and it begins to prepare.

The first preparation is to copy the instructions. Inside the nucleus, the DNA holds the entire recipe book. Before the cell splits, every page of that book has to be duplicated, so that each daughter cell gets a complete copy. This copying is careful and slow. Special protein machines unzip the DNA, read each strand, and build a matching strand against it. By the time the copying is done, the cell has two complete sets of instructions where it had one before.

Each set is bundled into structures called chromosomes. In a human cell there are forty-six chromosomes, but after copying there are still forty-six — each one is now a doubled chromosome, two identical strands joined at the middle, looking under a microscope a little like a small letter X. The cell now has to separate these doubled chromosomes so that one of each pair ends up on each side.

This separation is the showpiece of cell division. The nuclear membrane dissolves. A scaffolding called the spindle reaches out from two opposite ends of the cell, made of long protein fibers that grow toward the middle and attach to each chromosome. Then the fibers pull. Slowly, the two halves of every doubled chromosome are tugged apart, one half traveling to one end of the cell and the other half to the other end. By the time the pulling is finished, each end of the cell has a complete set of forty-six chromosomes, identical to the set the original cell started with.

Now the cell pinches in the middle. The membrane and the cytoskeleton work together to draw a kind of belt around the equator of the cell, tightening it like the neck of a balloon. The pinch deepens until the two halves separate. A fresh nuclear membrane forms around each new set of chromosomes. The cytoplasm, with its mitochondria and other organelles, has been roughly split between the two daughters. There are now two cells where there was one. Each is smaller than the original was at its peak, but each is complete, and each will grow until it is the size the parent was.

This process is called mitosis when it makes ordinary body cells. From the outside it looks simple — one cell, then two — but it is one of the most dramatic things a cell ever does. There are checkpoints all the way through. If the DNA is damaged, the cell pauses to repair it before copying. If the spindle is not lined up properly, the cell waits. If something has gone seriously wrong, the cell may even kill itself rather than divide and pass on a mistake. This careful self-policing is one of the reasons we usually stay healthy. When the policing fails and a cell divides without checking, you get unrestrained growth, which is what cancer is.

There is a second kind of cell division, used only to make eggs and sperm, called meiosis. Meiosis is similar to mitosis at the start, but it splits the chromosome count in half rather than keeping it the same. An egg or a sperm carries only twenty-three chromosomes, half a set. When an egg and a sperm join, the new cell has the full forty-six again, half from one parent and half from the other. This is why you have features from each of your parents. Cell division is also how families keep going across generations.

Cell division is the reason a single fertilized egg can become a full person. That first cell divides into two, those into four, those into eight, and so on. Within a few weeks the count is in the millions, and the cells start to specialize, some becoming heart, some becoming brain, some becoming bone. Every cell in your body, all thirty trillion of them, can trace its lineage back through divisions to that first cell. You are a tree of cells, branching across time, and the branches are still growing.
