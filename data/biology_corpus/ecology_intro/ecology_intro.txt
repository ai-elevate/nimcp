Ecology: the study of living together.

Stand at the edge of a tide pool when the sea has just gone out, and look down. You will see a bowl of water surrounded by rocks, and inside that bowl a small world is alive. There are limpets clamped to the stone, anemones with their tentacles spread, tiny shrimp darting through the seaweed, a starfish folded against the wall. None of these creatures lives alone. The starfish eats the limpets. The limpets graze on the algae growing on the rock. The algae take energy from sunlight and minerals from the salt water. The shrimp eat bits of dead matter that drift down through the water and are themselves eaten by small fish that come in with the tide. Take any one creature out of the pool and the others would shift in response. Each one is part of something larger. The study of those larger somethings is called ecology.

The simplest unit ecology looks at is the population. A population is all the individuals of one species in one place. All the limpets in your tide pool. All the deer in a particular forest. All the people in a particular city. Populations have sizes, and those sizes change over time. They grow when births and arrivals exceed deaths and departures. They shrink when the reverse happens. They can crash quickly when food runs short or a disease sweeps through. A population can also be limited by the size of its space, the amount of food, the number of predators waiting nearby. Tracking why a population is the size it is, and why it changes, is one of the basic tasks of ecology.

Populations of different species share the same place, and that shared place is called a community. The community in your tide pool includes the population of limpets, the population of starfish, the population of anemones, the population of algae, the bacteria you cannot see, the eggs floating in the water. The community in a forest includes oaks, mosses, deer, hawks, beetles, soil fungi, and a thousand other things. A community is not a list of populations sitting next to each other. It is a network of interactions. Every species in a community is connected to many others through what it eats, what eats it, what it builds with, what it shelters in.

The most important of those connections is who eats whom. If you draw lines on a piece of paper for every eating relationship in your tide pool — limpet eats algae, starfish eats limpet, sea slug eats anemone, fish eats shrimp — you get a tangled web. This is called a food web. At the bottom of the web are the producers, almost always something green like algae or plants, which take energy directly from the sun. Above them are the herbivores, which eat the producers. Above those are the carnivores, which eat the herbivores, and above them sometimes a top predator, which eats other carnivores. Energy flows up through the web from the sun, through the green layer, through the eaters, with quite a lot lost as heat at every step. Most of what we call life is, at root, a way of moving sun-energy through bodies.

Inside this web, every species has a particular role, called its niche. The niche of an oak tree in a forest is to be the tall, slow, sunlight-catching producer that thousands of other species depend on. The niche of an earthworm is to chew through soil, breaking down dead plant matter and aerating the ground. The niche of a wolf is to hunt large herbivores, keeping the deer and elk in check. Two species cannot, for long, occupy exactly the same niche in the same community. If they tried, one would do it slightly better than the other and the other would be pushed out, or the two would shift their habits and divide the niche between them. This is one of the rules of community ecology, and it is why a healthy ecosystem usually has many species each doing something a little different.

Beyond the community is the ecosystem, which is the community plus the non-living things it depends on — the water, the soil, the rocks, the air, the sunlight. An ecosystem is a closed loop of materials and an open flow of energy. The carbon in a leaf will eventually find its way back into the air or the soil, then back into another leaf, and so on, in a cycle that has been turning for billions of years. The water in your tide pool comes in with the tide and goes out, carrying salts and nutrients in and waste matter out. The energy from the sun pours in continually and is finally lost as heat. Around these cycles and flows, communities of living things weave their lives.

Ecosystems can be small, like a single rotting log filled with beetles and fungi, or vast, like a coral reef or a rainforest. The largest of all is the whole biosphere, the thin film of life covering the planet from the deep sea to the high atmosphere. Every organism on Earth, including you, is part of it. The breath you just took included molecules that were inside a tree last year, inside a fish a century ago, perhaps inside a dinosaur a hundred million years before that. The atoms keep cycling. The communities keep shifting. The flow of energy keeps coming in from the sun.

When ecology goes wrong, you can usually trace it to one of these patterns being broken. Cut down all the producers in a region and the herbivores starve, then the carnivores. Kill off a top predator and the herbivores explode, eating the producers down to nothing. Add a new species from the other side of the world and it may have no predators in its new home and run away unchecked. Pour pollution into the water and you change the chemistry of the producers' lives, which travels up every layer of the web. The habits of the planet's communities are old and intricate. They can be quite robust, but they can also be broken faster than they can rebuild.

When you stand at that tide pool and watch a starfish slowly creep over a rock, you are looking at one move in a long, slow game played by the whole community. The pieces are alive. The rules are written in the food web, the niche, the cycles of matter, the flows of energy. Ecology is the patient study of that game, and the world it describes is the same world you live in, on a slightly bigger scale.
