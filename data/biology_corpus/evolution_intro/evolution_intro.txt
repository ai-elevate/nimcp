Evolution: how living things change.

If you take an old photograph of a wolf and a photograph of a chihuahua, and place them side by side, it is hard to believe one is descended from the other. Yet every dog on Earth, from the great dane to the toy poodle, descended from wolves. The change happened in the last twenty thousand years or so, fast on the timescale of life. People bred dogs, choosing wolves and then dogs that were friendlier, smaller, more obedient, fluffier, with floppy ears, with the markings they liked, generation after generation. The result is the dog. The wolf is still out there, mostly unchanged. The dog is what happens when you steer heredity in a direction.

Evolution is the same process, except that nature does the steering instead of a breeder. Most of the time, the steering is not steering at all but a kind of filtering. Every population of living things produces more offspring than can possibly survive. Some of those offspring are a little better suited to where they live than others. Those tend to live, and to have offspring of their own. The ones less suited tend not to. Across many generations, the traits of the survivors become the traits of the species, and the species drifts in the direction of fitness with its world. Charles Darwin called this natural selection. Today we call it evolution by natural selection.

To see how this works, picture a population of moths in a forest. The moths land on tree bark to rest during the day. The bark is a mottled gray, and most of the moths happen to be a similar color. They are hard for birds to see against the bark. A few moths are darker than usual, a few lighter. Birds eat the ones that stand out. The mottled-gray ones survive in slightly greater numbers, mate, and pass their gray color to the next generation. The population stays gray, and there is nothing dramatic happening — until something in the world changes.

Suppose factories nearby start belching black soot, and over a few years the bark on every tree turns dark. Now it is the gray moths that stand out, and the dark moths that blend in. Birds eat more grays than darks. The dark moths leave more offspring, and within a few decades the population of moths in that forest is mostly dark. This actually happened in England in the 1800s, with a moth called the peppered moth. It is one of the cleanest demonstrations of evolution in action. Nothing about any individual moth changed during its life. The moths did not work to become darker. They were already varied, and the world filtered them.

This is the heart of how evolution works. Three things must be true. First, individuals must vary; not every moth is the same shade. Second, that variation must be at least partly heritable; gray parents tend to have gray offspring, dark have dark. Third, some variants must do better at surviving and reproducing than others, in the world they happen to live in. Whenever these three things are true, the population will shift over generations toward whatever works in that world. Variation, heredity, differential survival. From these three ingredients, all the splendor of life eventually flows.

The variation comes from many sources. New offspring are a shuffle of their parents' genes, so siblings differ from each other. Sometimes the copying of DNA makes a small mistake, and a fresh variation appears that neither parent had. Most such mistakes do nothing useful. A few break things and lead to disease. A very few happen to be helpful, and those tend to spread because their carriers thrive. Evolution does not aim for a goal. It does not try to improve anything. It is a slow accumulation of accidents that worked.

Given enough time, the small changes add up to enormous differences. A hundred generations are barely noticeable in most species; a million generations can rebuild a body plan. Whales descended from four-legged land animals that walked into the shallows, ate fish, and over tens of millions of years became creatures that never leave the sea. Their hands are still there inside their flippers, with bones that match the bones of a dog's paw. Their hips are still there, vestigial, hidden inside the body, useless for anything but a clue to where they came from. Birds descended from a particular branch of dinosaurs and still wear the scaly feet of their ancestors under feathers that grew slowly more elaborate, generation after generation, until flight became possible. The fossil record shows the steps. The DNA confirms the relationships.

Every species alive today shares an ancestor with every other species alive today, somewhere back in deep time. You are related to a chimpanzee through an ancestor that lived about six million years ago. You are related to a mouse through an ancestor about ninety million years back. You are related to a tree through an ancestor that lived more than a billion years ago, when the only life on Earth was single cells. The whole tree of life is, literally, a tree, with branches that split again and again as populations diverged into new species, and with you on one of the leaves.

A common misunderstanding is that evolution is moving toward something. It is not. There is no goal, no ladder of progress with humans on top. There is only what works at any given moment in any given place. A bacterium living in your gut today is not less evolved than you are; it has been evolving for the same length of time, exactly as much, and in its particular niche it is exquisitely fitted. Evolution is a tree, not a ladder. The branches go in every direction. The leaves on every branch are equally current.

Once you see life this way, much of biology starts to make sense. Why do mammals have backbones? Because their distant ancestors did, and the body plan was kept and modified. Why do many flowers smell sweet? Because they evolved alongside bees and other pollinators that respond to scent. Why does a baby reach for its mother's face from the first day? Because in our ancestral past, the babies who recognized their mothers got more care and survived. The reasons in biology are nearly always histories. To understand a living thing, you ask how its ancestors lived, what filtered them, what they passed on. The answers are evolution.
