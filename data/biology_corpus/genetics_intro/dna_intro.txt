DNA: the molecule that remembers.

If you scrape a few cells from the inside of your cheek with the tip of your tongue, you are scraping off, among other things, a small amount of an extraordinary molecule. It is so thin you cannot see one strand of it under any ordinary microscope. Yet a single thread of it, taken from one of your cells and stretched out without breaking, would reach from your shoulder almost to your fingertip. There are about thirty trillion of these threads in your body, one set in nearly every cell. Their name is DNA, which stands for deoxyribonucleic acid, and they are the molecule that remembers what you are.

DNA carries the instructions for building and running a living thing. The instructions for making your eyes blue or brown, for shaping the curve of your ears, for telling your liver how to detoxify alcohol, for laying out the wiring of your brain — all of that is written into the DNA that was passed down to you when your mother's egg met your father's sperm. From that moment, every cell of your growing body inherited a copy. Every cell still has it, kept safe in the nucleus, and consults it whenever it needs to do something.

The molecule itself has a beautiful structure. Imagine a very long ladder. Two long rails run down the sides, and rungs cross between them. Now twist the ladder gently, like wringing out a wet cloth, so that it spirals around its own length. That spiral is called a double helix. It is the shape of DNA. The two rails are made of repeating units of sugar and phosphate, joined into long strings. The rungs of the ladder are made of pairs of small molecules called bases.

There are four bases, and they are usually written by the first letters of their names: A, T, G, and C. They have a strict pairing rule. A always pairs with T across the ladder, and G always pairs with C. So if you read down one rail of the ladder and see ATGGCT, you can be certain that the matching stretch on the other rail reads TACCGA. Every rung is one of these complementary pairs, an A-T or a G-C. The order of the bases is the message. Like the letters in a sentence, the order is what matters.

The order is enormous. A single human cell has about three billion of these letter-pairs. If you typed them out at one letter per second, day and night without stopping, it would take you about a hundred years to type your DNA once. The book of you is bigger than any library. And yet that whole book fits, folded almost beyond imagination, into the nucleus of a single cell, a space smaller than a grain of dust.

How does the cell read this enormous book? Not all at once. When a cell needs to make a particular protein — say, a digestive enzyme, or a hair pigment — it finds the matching gene, the relevant chapter, and copies just that chapter into a smaller, working copy. The smaller copy is made of a closely related molecule called RNA, which can leave the nucleus and travel out into the cytoplasm where the protein factories, called ribosomes, are waiting. The ribosome reads the RNA three letters at a time. Each three-letter combination tells it which amino acid to add next to the growing protein. A long string of amino acids, folded up into a particular shape, is what a protein is. Proteins do most of the work in a living thing.

So the chain runs like this. DNA stores the instructions. A gene is a stretch of DNA that codes for one protein. To use a gene, the cell makes an RNA copy. The RNA is read by a ribosome, which builds the protein. The protein then does its job — perhaps it digests a sugar, perhaps it carries oxygen, perhaps it helps a nerve fire. Multiply this by the thousands of different proteins in a cell, all being made and used at carefully controlled rates, and you have the chemistry of a living cell.

Why does the ladder shape matter? Because the strict pairing rule turns DNA into a self-copying molecule. When the cell needs to divide, it unzips the ladder down the middle, separating the two rails. Each rail then serves as a template. The cell's machinery brings free bases over and matches them to the exposed letters: an A finds a T, a G finds a C, until each rail has rebuilt its missing partner. Where there was one ladder there are now two, each identical to the original. This is how cells copy their DNA every time they divide, and this is how parents pass DNA on to their children.

The DNA in your cells is almost the same as the DNA in any other person, and quite similar to the DNA in a chimpanzee, a mouse, or a banana. Most of your DNA is shared with most other living things, because life on Earth has a single common ancestor and the same machinery has been passed down and slightly modified for billions of years. The differences between you and the person sitting next to you, between humans and apes, between animals and plants, all live inside the small percentage of letters where the books differ. And those differences, written in the same four letters, are why every living thing is different from every other.

DNA is, in the end, a chemical with a purpose. It does not move on its own, it does not think, it does not plan. But the order of its letters carries a message, and that message has been passed down, copy after copy, for billions of years. When a child is born, an unbroken thread of copied DNA stretches back from that child through every ancestor who ever lived. You are part of that thread. And in your cells, right now, the molecule is still there, still being read, still being copied, still remembering what you are.
