Why children look like their parents.

Look at a family photograph at a holiday table. The grandfather has a strong nose, and so does his son, and so does his grandson. The grandmother has eyes of a particular green, and one of her granddaughters has the same green, and the other has the deep brown of the boy she married. There is a chin, a way of laughing, a tendency to early baldness, that runs through the men of one branch but not the other. These resemblances are not coincidence. They are heredity at work.

Heredity is the simple-sounding fact that living things tend to make offspring that resemble them. A pair of golden retrievers will have golden retriever puppies, not poodle puppies. A wheat plant will produce seeds that grow into more wheat plants, not corn. People will have human children, and those children will resemble their two parents far more than they resemble strangers. Across all of life on Earth, this pattern holds. Parents pass something along to their children, and that something carries the recipe for the child's body.

Before anyone knew what that something was, people knew it had to exist. Farmers had been using heredity for thousands of years before there was a word for it. They would notice that one cow gave more milk than the others, and they would breed her with their best bull, and her calves would tend to give more milk too. They would save seeds from the wheat plants that had the largest grains, and the next year's crop would be a little better. By doing this carefully over many generations, ancient farmers turned a wild grass into the wheat we eat, turned a small bitter fruit into the apple, and turned a wolf into the hundreds of different breeds of dog. They did not know how heredity worked, but they trusted that it did, and they were right.

The first person to take heredity apart and look at how it worked, in a careful experimental way, was a monk named Gregor Mendel, working in a garden in the 1860s. He grew pea plants. He noticed that some of his pea plants had purple flowers and some had white, that some grew tall and some short, that some made smooth peas and some wrinkled. He took plants of one kind and crossed them with plants of the other kind, and he counted everything.

Mendel discovered something strange. When he crossed a tall plant with a short plant, he did not get medium-sized children. He got tall children. The shortness seemed to vanish — but only seemed to. When he then crossed the tall children with each other, the shortness came back, in about a quarter of the grandchildren. This pattern repeated everywhere he looked. Some traits hid for a generation and then reappeared in the next, in a regular ratio.

To explain this, Mendel proposed that each trait was carried by a pair of factors — one from each parent — and that some factors were stronger than others. A tall factor combined with a short factor would produce a tall plant, because tall is stronger; that is what is meant by a dominant factor. The short factor was hidden, but still there, and could be passed to the next generation. If two such hidden factors met up in a grandchild, the plant would be short.

Mendel was right, but his work was ignored for forty years. When it was rediscovered, around 1900, scientists realized that Mendel's factors had a physical home. Each cell of a plant or animal contained tiny structures called chromosomes, and these chromosomes were inherited from the parents in pairs, exactly the way Mendel said his factors were. The factors became known as genes, and the chromosomes were where the genes lived.

A gene is, at heart, a piece of instruction. It is a stretch of a long molecule called DNA, which we will look at more closely in another lesson. The instruction tells the cell how to make a particular protein, or how to do a particular thing. A gene for eye color tells certain cells how much pigment to make in the iris. A gene for height influences how much the bones grow. A gene for blood type tells the body which markers to put on the surface of red blood cells. Every cell in your body carries all your genes, but in any given cell only some of them are switched on; a liver cell uses different genes than a brain cell, even though both have the full set.

You inherited your genes from your parents. Half came from your mother, in the egg, and half came from your father, in the sperm. The two halves combined when you were conceived, and that combination was the first version of you. From that point on, every time one of your cells divided, it copied all of those genes and gave a full set to the daughter cell. So every cell in your body, except a few that throw their nucleus away, carries the same library of genes that was assembled at the moment you were conceived.

That is why children look like their parents, and yet are not exactly like either. They are a fresh shuffle of the parents' decks. A child may have her mother's eyes, her father's nose, an unusual ear-shape from a great-grandmother. The shuffle gives every individual a slightly new combination, a new variation on the family theme. And it is from this small variation, multiplied across generations and acted on by the world, that the great patterns of life on Earth eventually arise.
