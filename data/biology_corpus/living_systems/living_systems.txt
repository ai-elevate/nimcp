What it means to be alive.

A rock and a mouse are both made of atoms. Both are sitting in the same kitchen, in the same air. Yet one of them is alive and the other is not, and you can tell the difference at a glance. What is it that the mouse has and the rock does not?

This question has been asked for a long time, and the answer is not a single thing but a list of behaviors. Living things do certain things that non-living things do not, and they keep doing them as long as they are alive. Stop those behaviors and the living thing dies. Start them up in something that lacks them and you have not seen them in nature, only in stories.

The first behavior is taking in matter and energy from outside, and using it to keep going. A mouse breathes air, drinks water, and eats seeds. It uses oxygen and sugar to power its muscles, its heart, its brain. A rock just sits there. If you take away its surroundings the rock does not change. Take away the mouse's air and water and food and it will be dead within minutes, hours, or days, depending on which one you remove. Living things are open systems. They have to keep pulling energy through themselves to stay assembled.

The second behavior is using that energy to maintain a kind of ordered chemistry inside. A mouse keeps its temperature near thirty-seven degrees, its blood salts in a narrow range, its acidity carefully balanced. If any of these slip too far, the mouse gets sick or dies. The mouse does not check these levels with a thermometer; its body has its own quiet system of thermostats and pumps that nudge things back toward the proper values. This staying-in-balance is called homeostasis, from Greek words meaning standing-the-same. Even when the world around it is freezing or sweltering, the mouse holds its inside steady.

The third behavior is responding to the world. Clap your hands and the mouse will jump. Shine a light and it will run for cover. Drop a crumb and it will come and sniff. The rock does none of these things. To respond, the mouse has senses — eyes, ears, nose, whiskers — and a nervous system that takes the sense data and produces an action. Even very simple living things do this, in their own way. A bacterium will swim toward sugar and away from acid. A plant will turn its leaves toward the sun. The detail varies, but the pattern is the same: living things detect their world and act on it.

The fourth behavior is growing. A mouse starts as a tiny embryo no bigger than a poppy seed and grows over weeks into a full-sized animal. A tree starts as a seed and becomes a hundred-foot living tower. To grow, a living thing has to take the food it eats and reorganize the atoms into more of itself. This is one of the strangest things about life. A mouse eats wheat. The wheat is not mouse. Yet the mouse turns the wheat into mouse, breaks the seed apart at the molecular level and reassembles the parts as muscle, fur, liver, blood. A rock cannot do this. Pour sand on a rock and you have a rock with sand on it.

The fifth behavior is reproducing. A mouse will, in time, have baby mice. A tree will produce seeds. A bacterium will divide into two bacteria. Without reproduction, a species would last only as long as its current members lived, and life on Earth would have ended with whatever creatures came first. Because of reproduction, life passes itself forward, and every living thing on Earth today is the descendant of an unbroken chain of living things going back at least three and a half billion years.

There is a sixth behavior, which is harder to see in any individual but stands out across many generations. Living things change. Each new generation is a little different from the one before, sometimes by chance, sometimes because the world has changed and only certain individuals managed to thrive in the new conditions. Over many generations these changes add up, and species can shift, split, and turn into new species. We will return to this in lessons on evolution.

All living things share the basic needs. They need water, because the chemistry of life happens in salty water. They need a source of energy. For animals like the mouse, the energy comes from food, which is the bodies and products of other living things. For plants, the energy comes from sunlight, captured by a green chemical called chlorophyll and stored as sugar. For some bacteria, the energy comes from chemicals deep in the rock or in hot springs at the bottom of the sea. Different paths, but every living thing must have a way to bring energy in.

They also need a way to stay protected. A mouse has skin, fur, and a behavior of hiding. A plant has bark, thorns, and chemicals in its leaves that taste bad to grazers. A bacterium has a tough wall around its cell. Living things are fragile in their delicate inner chemistry, and they invest a great deal in not being eaten or dried out or frozen.

The list is long, but it can be summarized as a single line. To be alive is to keep yourself going against the world's tendency to fall apart, by drawing energy through yourself, by sensing and reacting, by repairing what wears out, and by passing on what you have learned to the next generation. The rock does none of this. The mouse does all of it, every second of its short busy life.
