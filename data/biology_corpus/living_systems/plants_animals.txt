Plants and animals: two ways of being alive.

If you walk through a meadow on a summer afternoon, almost everything you see that is alive falls into one of two great groups. There are animals — the bees, the rabbits, the birds, the worms, you yourself — and there are plants, the grass under your feet, the trees at the edge of the field, the wildflowers nodding in the breeze. They look different. They behave differently. Yet they are deeply related, and understanding how they differ tells you something about what life can be.

The first and biggest difference is how they get their food. A rabbit eats grass. A bee drinks nectar. A robin eats worms. Animals are eaters. They survive by taking the bodies of other living things into themselves and breaking them down for energy and raw materials. To eat, you have to find food and catch it, so animals tend to move. They have legs or wings or fins, eyes, ears, a nervous system to coordinate the chase. Even slow animals like clams move at least a little. The whole shape of an animal — fast metabolism, strong muscles, sharp senses — is built around the problem of finding the next meal.

A plant does not need to find food. It makes its own. A plant takes water from the soil through its roots, takes carbon dioxide from the air through tiny holes in its leaves, and uses the energy of sunlight to combine these into sugar. This astonishing trick is called photosynthesis. The green substance in a leaf, called chlorophyll, captures sunlight the way a solar panel captures it. The leaf then uses that captured energy to do chemistry that no animal can do — to assemble carbon dioxide and water into sugar, the same sugar an animal would have to eat to live.

Because a plant carries its food factory inside its own leaves, it does not need to chase anything. It can sit in one place for a hundred years and still eat well, as long as the sun shines and the rain falls. So plants do not have legs. They do not have eyes or ears, in the way animals do. They have roots that grow downward, leaves that spread upward, and not much in the middle that needs to move. Their lives are slow and rooted. Where animals are travelers, plants are residents.

This single difference — eaters versus makers — drives most of the others. An animal needs a mouth, a stomach, a way to break down food. A plant has neither. An animal has muscles, which are protein and require constant fuel. A plant has wood, which is mostly cellulose and lignin, dead structural material that costs nothing to maintain. An animal has a nervous system to coordinate fast movement. A plant has a much slower communication system based on chemicals seeping from one part to another, which is fast enough for the kind of life it leads. An animal usually breathes oxygen in and breathes carbon dioxide out. A plant does both, but the dominant flow during the day is the opposite — it takes carbon dioxide in to make sugar and lets oxygen out as a waste product.

This last fact is one of the most important things on Earth. The oxygen you are breathing right now was put into the air by plants and other photosynthesizing creatures, going back hundreds of millions of years. Without plants there would be no animals, because there would be no oxygen for animals to breathe and no food for animals to eat. Animals depend on plants for almost everything that keeps them alive. Plants do not, in the same way, need animals — though many plants now use animals to spread their pollen and their seeds, which is a partnership that grew up over time.

Both groups, despite their differences, are made of cells. Plant cells and animal cells share the same basic layout: a membrane, a nucleus with DNA, mitochondria, the works. Where they differ is in two extra features that plant cells have. Plant cells have chloroplasts, the green machines that do photosynthesis, which animal cells lack. And plant cells have a stiff outer wall, made of cellulose, which sits outside the membrane and gives the plant its rigid structure. That stiff wall is why a tree can stand a hundred feet tall and a person cannot. Animals replace stiffness with bones and tense muscles; plants build it into the wall of every cell.

There are creatures that blur the line, of course. There are tiny green specks in the ocean that drift like animals but photosynthesize like plants. There are fungi, which used to be lumped with plants but are actually closer to animals — they cannot photosynthesize, they live by absorbing nutrients from the world around them, and they grow more like animals do at the cellular level. The mushroom you see is just the tip; most of the fungus is a spreading underground network. Bacteria, smaller still, do everything imaginable. The two-group picture of life is a starting point, not the whole truth.

Still, plants and animals are the two great kingdoms you meet in everyday life. You eat them both. You depend on them both. Walk through that meadow again and notice how everything green is rooted and still, and everything that moves is brown or gray or feathered. You are watching two complete strategies for being alive, sharing the same field, each making a living in its own way.
