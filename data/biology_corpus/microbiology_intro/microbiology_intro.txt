The unseen majority: bacteria, viruses, and the small life around us.

When you bake a loaf of bread, you mix flour and water and salt, and you add a small lump of dough left over from a previous loaf or a pinch of dry yeast from a packet. You wait a few hours, and the dough rises and grows soft and bubbly. The pinch of yeast did this. Yeast is a tiny single-celled fungus, and given sugar and warmth and time, every yeast cell eats some of the sugar in the flour, breathes out a small puff of carbon dioxide, and divides into two yeast cells. Multiply that process by billions of cells over a few hours and you get bubbles all through the dough. When you bake the bread, the heat kills the yeast and the bubbles freeze in place, and the result is the soft, holey crumb of a fresh loaf.

The yeast did not know it was making your bread. It was just making more of itself, the way it has been doing for hundreds of millions of years. Every time you eat sourdough or drink beer or wine, you are eating or drinking the byproducts of microbes living their tiny lives. There is a whole world of these creatures that we cannot see without a microscope, and learning about them is a branch of biology called microbiology.

The simplest and most numerous of microbes are bacteria. A bacterium is a single cell, much smaller than the cells of your body, with no nucleus. Its DNA floats free in the cell, usually as a single circular loop, along with simpler organelles than the ones in animal cells. Most bacteria are roughly oval or rod-shaped, sometimes with a long whip called a flagellum that they spin to swim. They are unimaginably small and unimaginably common. There are more bacteria living in a teaspoon of garden soil than there are people on Earth. There are more bacteria living on and inside your body than there are cells of your body.

Most bacteria do not bother you. Many help you. The bacteria that live in your gut — your gut microbiome — break down kinds of food your own digestive system cannot handle, make vitamins your body needs, and crowd out more dangerous bugs. There are bacteria in the soil that take nitrogen out of the air and turn it into forms that plants can use, which is the only reason there is enough nitrogen in the soil for crops to grow. There are bacteria deep in the sea that eat the chemicals coming out of hot vents and make a base for entire ecosystems where no sunlight reaches. Bacteria are recyclers and quiet partners across most of the planet, and life as we know it could not run without them.

A few bacteria do cause disease. Strep throat, tetanus, tuberculosis, and cholera are all caused by bacteria. Until the discovery of antibiotics in the early twentieth century, even small infections could be deadly. A scratch could turn into blood poisoning. Childbirth often killed mothers from infection. The discovery that certain molecules — first penicillin, harvested from a mold — could kill bacteria without killing people was one of the great turning points of medicine. Antibiotics still save lives every day, but bacteria evolve resistance to them, and using antibiotics carelessly speeds that evolution along, which is why doctors are now careful about when to prescribe them.

Even smaller than bacteria are viruses, and they are not exactly alive. A virus is a tiny package of genetic instructions — DNA or RNA — wrapped in a coat of protein, sometimes with a thin envelope of fat. It cannot eat. It cannot grow on its own. It cannot reproduce on its own. It does only one thing: it gets inside a cell and hijacks the cell's machinery to make copies of itself. The cell, busy turning out new viruses, eventually bursts or wears out, and the new viruses spread to other cells. Most of what we call a viral disease — a cold, the flu, chickenpox, measles — is the body's response to viruses replicating inside its cells.

Because viruses are not alive in the usual sense, antibiotics do not touch them. To fight a virus, the body relies on the immune system, and modern medicine relies mainly on vaccines, which teach the immune system to recognize a virus before it ever arrives. There are also antiviral drugs that interfere with specific steps of viral replication, but they are usually narrower and harder to make than antibiotics.

Beyond bacteria and viruses there are other microbes. Fungi, like the yeast that raises your bread or the molds that ripen blue cheese, are a third great kingdom of life, neither plant nor animal. Some fungi are huge — a single fungus in Oregon spreads underground over more than three square miles — but most are small or invisible. Single-celled organisms called protists include the amoeba, slipper-shaped paramecium, and many parasites that cause diseases like malaria. The microbial world is wildly more diverse than the world of large life.

Microbes also cooperate, often invisibly. The bacteria in your gut produce molecules that affect your mood. The bacteria in soil partner with the roots of plants to exchange nutrients. The bacteria in a cow's stomach do most of the work of digesting grass; the cow could not survive without them. The fermentation that turns milk into cheese, cabbage into sauerkraut, soybeans into miso, grapes into wine — every one of those traditional foods is the work of a community of microbes living in the food and changing it for you.

When you wash your hands, you are sweeping away thousands of microbes, mostly harmless, some not. When you finish a course of antibiotics, you have wiped out a chunk of your own gut community, and it will take weeks or months to fully come back. When you breathe in, you draw in microbes that you will probably never notice. Most of life on Earth is microbial. The plants and animals you can see are only the visible tip. Below them, around them, inside them, the unseen majority is living its tiny lives, eating, breathing, dividing, and shaping the world we share.
