The immune system: a defense that learns.

Cut your finger on the edge of a piece of paper. It barely hurts. A tiny bead of blood comes up, and you wipe it away and keep working. Within a few minutes the bleeding has stopped. Within an hour the cut is sealed by a thin scab. Within a few days the skin has knitted itself back together with no scar. You did not take any medicine. You did not even think about it. Yet inside that small cut, a complicated battle was fought and won, on your behalf, by your immune system.

Every break in the skin lets in something that should not be there. The paper that cut you carried bacteria. So did the air, your clothes, the desk. Some of those bacteria would happily multiply inside the warm wet world of your tissues, given the chance. Most never get the chance, because the immune system spots them and kills them faster than they can grow. The whole purpose of the immune system is to find things that are not you and remove them, while leaving you alone.

The first line of defense is the skin and the linings of the body. Intact skin is a tough barrier; few germs can cross it. The mouth, the eyes, the nose, the airways, the digestive tract are coated in mucus that traps invaders, and washed by tears or saliva or stomach acid that kills many of them. These barriers do enormous work without ever being noticed. You breathe in thousands of bacteria and viruses every day. Almost all of them are caught in the nose or burned away in the stomach. Only the ones that slip past the barriers ever meet the more sophisticated parts of the immune system.

When something does get past, it meets the first wave of defenders, which are general-purpose cells that attack anything they recognize as foreign. The most common are white blood cells called neutrophils, which patrol the blood and the tissues and rush to the site of any injury or infection. They engulf bacteria, swallowing them whole, and break them apart inside themselves with chemical weapons. Other cells, called macrophages, do the same and also clean up debris. There are dendritic cells whose job is to grab a sample of the invader and run with it to the lymph nodes, where the more careful learning machinery lives. There are inflammation signals — the redness, swelling, warmth, and pain that come with any injury — caused by chemicals these cells release to call in reinforcements.

This first wave is fast but not very picky. It treats most invaders the same and is mostly the same response your great-grandparents had, and their great-grandparents, going back to ancient ancestors. It is called the innate immune system. It is excellent at catching the obvious. But the world is full of pathogens that are subtle, that disguise themselves, that change between infections. To handle these, you need a defense that learns. That defense is the adaptive immune system.

The adaptive immune system is built around two kinds of cells, called B cells and T cells, both of them lymphocytes, both made in the bone marrow. Each B cell or T cell carries on its surface a receptor that is shaped to fit one particular pattern. Different B cells have differently shaped receptors. Across all the B and T cells in your body, you have receptors for an astonishing number of possible patterns — far more than the number of pathogens that exist. Almost any invader, from any disease that ever existed or could ever exist, has at least a few of your B and T cells already shaped to fit it.

When a B cell or T cell with the right receptor finally meets the pattern it fits — its antigen — something dramatic happens. The cell starts to divide rapidly. From one matching cell come hundreds, then thousands, then millions of identical copies, all carrying the same receptor, all aimed at the same enemy. B cells release antibodies, free-floating Y-shaped proteins that stick to the invader and mark it for destruction. T cells either kill infected cells directly or coordinate the rest of the immune response. The army that started as one or two specific cells becomes a focused, swarming response that can clear an infection in days.

What makes this system extraordinary is its memory. After the infection is over, most of the cloned cells die off, but a few stay behind as memory cells. They patrol your body for the rest of your life, ready to recognize the same invader if it comes again. The next time the same bug shows up, those memory cells respond within hours instead of days. You usually do not even feel it. This is why you only get chickenpox once. Your memory cells from your first bout watch for the virus forever after, and crush any new infection before it can take hold.

Vaccines work by training this memory without making you sick. A vaccine shows your immune system a piece of a pathogen — a weakened version of the virus, a fragment of its protein, a snippet of its genetic instructions — without ever giving you the dangerous part of the disease. Your B and T cells respond as if to a real infection, and lay down memory. Years later, when the real pathogen finally shows up, the memory is already there, and you fight it off without ever knowing.

Sometimes the immune system gets confused. If it mistakes some part of you for an invader, it attacks the wrong target, and you get an autoimmune disease. If it overreacts to something harmless like pollen or peanuts, you get an allergy. If it underreacts because of illness or medication, normally trivial infections become dangerous. The system is delicately calibrated. The line between you and not-you must be drawn correctly, or there is trouble.

When you scratch your finger and it heals on its own, you are watching the easy case — the small breach handled by the first wave, with no need for the more sophisticated response. When you survive a flu, you are watching the harder case — the adaptive system catching up, the antibodies and killer T cells doing their work, and the memory cells settling in for the years to come. The defense is invisible from outside, but it is one of the most active things going on inside you, every minute of every day, while you live your ordinary life and almost never think about it.
