The body as a system of systems.

Press your fingers against the side of your neck, just under your jaw. Within a few seconds you will feel a soft, regular thump — your pulse, the wave of pressure rolling along an artery every time your heart contracts. The thumping is happening about once a second, and has been happening about once a second since before you were born, without your attention or permission. It will keep happening for as long as you are alive. There is a lot going on inside you that you do not think about. Almost all of it is being managed by what biologists call body systems.

A body system is a group of organs that work together to do one big job. The job might be moving blood around, or digesting food, or sensing the world. No single organ does the job alone. The heart is just a pump; it needs blood vessels to carry the blood and lungs to refresh it with oxygen. A stomach by itself is not enough; it needs a mouth at one end, intestines at the other, and a liver and pancreas to help with the chemistry. So we group organs by the job they do together, and we call each group a system.

The circulatory system is what you felt in your neck. Its job is to move blood, and through the blood, to move everything else — oxygen from the lungs to the muscles, sugars from the digestive tract to the brain, hormones from the glands to wherever those messages need to land, waste products to the kidneys for disposal. The pump is the heart, a muscle about the size of your fist that beats roughly a hundred thousand times a day. The pipes are the blood vessels: arteries that carry blood out under pressure, veins that bring it back, and tiny capillaries so narrow that red blood cells must squeeze through them one at a time. If you laid all your blood vessels end to end, they would stretch around the equator more than twice. They reach into every part of you, because every cell needs the things blood carries.

Tied closely to the circulatory system is the respiratory system, whose job is breathing. You take air into your lungs, and inside the lungs the oxygen passes into your blood and the carbon dioxide passes out. The lungs are not a simple bag. They are a finely branched tree of tubes, ending in millions of tiny sacs called alveoli, each surrounded by capillaries. Spread out flat, the inside surface of your lungs would cover a tennis court. That is how much area you need for the gas exchange that keeps you alive. Every breath you take, even one you do not notice, is moving through that vast hidden landscape inside your chest.

The digestive system is a long tube that runs from your mouth to the other end. Food enters at the top, gets chewed and softened, slides down the esophagus into the stomach, and there is broken down by acid and enzymes. From the stomach, the partly-digested food moves into the small intestine, where the real chemistry happens. Enzymes from the pancreas and bile from the liver finish breaking the food into its smallest pieces — sugars, amino acids, fatty acids — and the lining of the intestine, which is folded and ridged into an enormous surface area, absorbs them into the blood. What is left passes into the large intestine, which reclaims most of the water before sending the rest along to be eliminated. A meal you eat today is being processed by this system for many hours after, while you go about your business unaware.

The nervous system is the body's wiring and central decision-maker. It includes the brain, the spinal cord, and the long thin nerves that reach into every organ, every muscle, every patch of skin. Sensory nerves carry information toward the brain — the brightness of a light, the pressure of a chair under you, the sound of a voice nearby. Motor nerves carry instructions back out to the muscles, telling them when to contract. The brain in the middle holds it all together, makes decisions, remembers, plans, dreams. The nervous system is the part of you that is most directly you. The other systems keep you running. This one is the running.

The musculoskeletal system gives you shape and motion. The skeleton, made of bones, is a rigid scaffold. The muscles, attached to the bones across joints, pull when they contract, and the bones lever your body around. Walking, lifting, smiling, breathing — all are muscles pulling on bones or on each other. Bones also store minerals, particularly calcium, and the marrow inside the bigger bones is where new blood cells are made. Bone is alive; it remodels itself in response to use, growing stronger where stress is greatest and being eaten away where it is not needed.

The immune system, which we will look at more closely in another lesson, is the body's defense network. It identifies and destroys things that should not be inside you — bacteria, viruses, dangerous substances, your own cells when they go bad. The endocrine system is a slower communication network than the nervous system, sending hormones through the blood to coordinate growth, metabolism, mood, and reproduction over hours or years rather than milliseconds. The urinary system filters the blood and disposes of waste. The reproductive system, when active, makes new humans. There are others. Each one focuses on a particular set of problems.

What is striking is how the systems interlock. The circulatory system carries oxygen from the respiratory system, sugar from the digestive system, hormones from the endocrine system, and immune cells from the immune system, all in the same liquid, all at the same time. The nervous system speaks to all of them, speeding the heart when you run, slowing your digestion when you are afraid, opening or closing the blood vessels in your skin to control your temperature. No system stands apart. The body is not a collection of separate machines but a single orchestra, with each section playing its part in time with the others.

The remarkable fact, when you stop and consider it, is that none of this requires your conscious effort. You did not learn to digest. You do not have to remember to make blood. You have a body that runs itself, mostly silently, mostly correctly, while you go about the business of being a person. The systems do their work, and your job is to keep yourself fed, watered, and rested enough that they can keep going.
