Some substances bite. A drop of lemon juice on a tongue tingles. Vinegar has a sharpness that smells the same way it tastes. The fizzing soda you drink is mildly aggressive in your mouth. These are all behaviors of the chemical family called acids.

Other substances feel slippery. A bit of soap dissolved in water has a slick, soapy feel between your fingers. Bleach has the same slippery quality. The cleaning solution under your sink, if you stick your hand in it accidentally, feels strangely soft because it is busy dissolving the oils on your skin. These are bases. Acids and bases are partners. They are opposites in the same way that addition and subtraction are opposites, and the same way that hot and cold are opposites. Each one undoes the other.

The chemistry behind the family resemblance is hydrogen. An acid is a substance that, when dissolved in water, releases hydrogen ions. A hydrogen ion, written H⁺, is just a hydrogen atom that has lost its single electron, leaving behind only the proton. Acids increase the number of free protons floating in water. The more protons are released, the stronger the acid. Vinegar releases a moderate number, so it is a moderate acid. Hydrochloric acid in your stomach releases many, so it is a strong one. Lemon juice is somewhere in between. The free protons are what your tongue detects as sourness.

A base does the opposite. A base reduces the number of free protons in solution, often by releasing hydroxide ions, written OH⁻. The hydroxide ion grabs onto any free proton and combines with it to form a water molecule. So adding a base to water uses up free protons. The more free protons get used up, the stronger the base. Soap is mildly basic. Bleach is more so. Lye, used in soap making, is one of the strongest household bases.

When you mix an acid and a base together, the H⁺ and the OH⁻ find each other and join into H₂O. The acidic punch and the basic slipperiness cancel out. What remains is water plus a salt. This pairing is called neutralization, and it is why baking soda, which is mildly basic, can soothe an acidic upset stomach, and why a splash of vinegar can take the smell of bleach off a kitchen counter. Two opposites combining into something tame.

To track the strength of acids and bases, chemists use a number called pH. The pH is a small number, usually between zero and fourteen. A pH of seven is neutral, like pure water. Numbers below seven are acidic, with smaller numbers meaning stronger acids. Numbers above seven are basic, with larger numbers meaning stronger bases. Vinegar sits around three. Lemon juice around two. Stomach acid around one. Coffee around five. Pure water at seven. Sea water around eight. Soap around nine or ten. Bleach around twelve. The scale is logarithmic, which means each step is ten times the previous step. A pH of three is ten times more acidic than a pH of four, and a hundred times more acidic than a pH of five.

Your body keeps a careful pH. The blood is held very close to a pH of seven point four, slightly basic, and only a tiny drift in either direction is dangerous. The saliva in your mouth is roughly neutral but shifts after meals. The inside of your stomach, by contrast, is dramatically acidic. The stomach acid is hydrochloric acid strong enough to dissolve bone, kept from dissolving the stomach itself by a thick layer of protective mucus. When the protective layer fails, you feel it as heartburn, and an antacid tablet works by neutralizing some of the acid with a basic ingredient like calcium carbonate or magnesium hydroxide.

Plants use pH information from the soil. Some plants thrive in slightly acidic soil. Some need it slightly basic. Hydrangea flowers, famously, change color depending on the pH of the soil they grow in. Acidic soil produces blue blooms, basic soil produces pink. The color is itself a pH indicator made by the plant.

Indicators are substances that change color depending on the pH around them. Litmus paper turns red in acid and blue in base. Red cabbage juice turns red in acid, purple at neutral, and green or yellow in strong base. You can boil red cabbage at home and use the strained water as a homemade pH indicator. Drop a bit into vinegar and watch it flush red. Drop a bit into baking soda solution and watch it turn green. The cabbage molecule responsible is sensitive to free protons; gain or lose a few from its structure and its color shifts.

The acid-base perspective also explains why your kitchen baking works. Baking soda, NaHCO₃, is a base. It releases CO₂ gas when it meets an acid. Recipes that call for baking soda almost always also call for an acidic ingredient, like buttermilk or yogurt or lemon juice. The two react. The CO₂ bubbles get trapped in the dough as it bakes, and the bread or cake rises. Without the acid, the baking soda barely reacts, and your bread is flat. The whole structure of leavened baking is built on acid-base chemistry.

So when you taste sourness, when you feel slipperiness, when you watch a cake rise, you are watching the same simple game. Atoms passing protons back and forth. The pH scale puts a number on the score, and that number quietly governs the chemistry of food, biology, and weather alike.
