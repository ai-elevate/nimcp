Everything you can touch is made of tiny pieces. Hold a small pebble in your hand. It feels solid and continuous, but if you could shrink down small enough, you would see it is built from countless little particles packed together. Those particles are called atoms. Atoms are the building blocks of every solid, every liquid, and every gas you have ever encountered.

An atom is unimaginably small. If you lined up ten million of them in a row, the row would still be shorter than the width of a single human hair. Yet a glass of water contains more atoms than there are stars in the visible universe. The world is fine-grained in a way that everyday vision cannot reveal.

Each atom has a tiny core called the nucleus, and around the nucleus there is a cloud of even tinier particles called electrons. The nucleus itself is made of two kinds of particles, protons and neutrons. Protons carry a positive electric charge. Electrons carry a negative electric charge. Neutrons, as the name hints, are electrically neutral. The protons and electrons attract each other, the way the north pole of one magnet attracts the south pole of another, and that attraction is what holds the atom together.

Imagine the atom as a tiny solar system, but tilt that picture in your mind, because it is only a sketch. The nucleus sits at the center the way the sun sits at the center of our solar system. The electrons swarm around the nucleus, but they do not move in tidy circles like planets. They live in fuzzy, layered clouds. Even so, the picture of a small dense center with light particles buzzing around it gets you started.

What makes one kind of atom different from another is the number of protons in its nucleus. An atom with one proton is hydrogen. An atom with six protons is carbon. An atom with eight protons is oxygen. An atom with twenty-six protons is iron. The proton count is the atom's identity card. Change that number and you change the element entirely. This is also why the alchemists of long ago could not turn lead into gold by mixing things in a pot. Mixing things rearranges atoms but does not change what is inside their nuclei.

Atoms rarely live alone. They join with each other to form molecules. A molecule is a small group of atoms held together by chemical bonds, the way two children hold hands and walk as a pair. Two hydrogen atoms can stick to one oxygen atom and form a water molecule, written H₂O. Two oxygen atoms can pair up and form the oxygen molecule O₂ that you breathe. Three oxygen atoms can stick together as ozone, O₃, the gas that protects the surface of the planet from harsh sunlight.

When you breathe in, you pull in trillions of molecules. Most of them are nitrogen, N₂, which is two nitrogen atoms bonded together. About one fifth are O₂. A tiny fraction are CO₂, carbon dioxide, which is one carbon atom with two oxygen atoms attached. When you breathe out, you push out more CO₂ than you pulled in, because your body has been quietly running a slow combustion of food and producing this molecule as a waste gas. Your breath is a chemistry report.

Notice how the same handful of atoms keeps appearing in different combinations. Hydrogen and oxygen alone make water, hydrogen peroxide, and many of the sugars and fats in your body. Carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen together make almost every food you have ever eaten. Carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen together make every protein. The richness of the world comes not from a huge variety of atoms, but from a small number of atoms combined in countless ways. Roughly a hundred elements exist in nature, and almost everything you see is built from fewer than thirty of them.

When you smell coffee, what reaches your nose are individual molecules that escaped from the cup, drifted across the room, and landed inside your nasal passages. When iron rusts, what is happening is that iron atoms at the surface of the metal are joining with oxygen atoms from the air to form a new substance, iron oxide. When ice melts, the water molecules are not changing, only their arrangement is loosening. Almost every change you notice in everyday life is, deep down, atoms rearranging themselves.

So when you look at the world, try this small reframing. Instead of seeing a wooden chair, a glass of water, and a piece of fruit, picture each as an enormous swarm of atoms, sorted into molecules, held together by tiny electric tugs. The chair, the glass, the fruit, and you yourself are all expressions of the same underlying inventory, arranged differently. That is the central idea of chemistry, and from here every other idea follows.
