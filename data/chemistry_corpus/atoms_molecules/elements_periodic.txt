There are roughly a hundred elements in nature. An element is a substance made of just one kind of atom. Pure iron is one element. Pure gold is another. Pure oxygen is another. If you took an element and tried to break it down by any chemical method, you would just end up with smaller piles of the same element. Elements are the simplest chemical substances we have.

The early chemists noticed something strange as they catalogued the elements. The list was not random. Certain elements behaved alike, almost as if nature had grouped them into families. Sodium and potassium are both soft, silvery metals. Drop a small piece of either into water and it fizzes violently and sometimes catches fire. Lithium does the same thing, only a little more gently. Three different metals, three very similar personalities. There must be a reason.

Chlorine and fluorine and bromine, on the other hand, are aggressive non-metals. They are pale colored gases or liquids that react eagerly with almost anything they meet. Each forms a salt when it meets sodium. Sodium chloride, the salt on your dinner table, is one example. Sodium fluoride, used in toothpaste, is another. Sodium bromide is yet another. Different non-metals, same kind of partnership with sodium.

Helium and neon and argon refuse to react with almost anything at all. They float around as single atoms, never bonding to form molecules, never burning, never rusting. They are aloof. The bright glowing tubes of neon signs are filled with one of these gases, set glowing only by an electric current passing through.

These family resemblances are not a coincidence. The Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev noticed that if you arrange the elements in order of increasing proton count, the families repeat at regular intervals. Sodium and potassium are seven and eight steps apart from lithium, in such a way that they line up neatly when you write the elements in rows of the right length. Mendeleev arranged the elements into a grid where the columns hold the families and the rows hold a complete cycle of properties. He called it the periodic table because the properties of the elements repeat periodically as you walk through them in order.

Mendeleev had so much faith in his table that when he found gaps where no known element fit, he did not flinch. He predicted that an element belonging in that gap must exist, and he predicted what its properties would be by reading the column above it. Years later, chemists found those missing elements, and they had almost exactly the properties he had predicted. The table was real. It was telling us something deep about the structure of matter.

The reason for the patterns turned out to involve the electrons. Recall that an atom has a cloud of electrons around its nucleus. Those electrons live in shells, and each shell can hold only so many electrons. Hydrogen has one electron in its single shell. Helium has two, which fills its shell completely. Lithium has three, so two fill the inner shell and the third is alone in the next shell out. Sodium has eleven, with one lone electron in the outermost shell. Potassium has nineteen, again with one lone outer electron. That lone outer electron is the explanation. Sodium and potassium and lithium all behave alike because they all have one outer electron eager to leave or to be shared.

Helium and neon and argon, by contrast, all have completely filled outer shells. They are content. They do not need to bond with anyone, so they do not. The chemistry of an atom is the chemistry of its outermost electrons.

The columns of the periodic table tell you about reactivity and bonding tendencies. The rows tell you about size and how far the outer shell sits from the nucleus. The position of an element in the table is, at a glance, a summary of how it will behave. A chemist who knows the table the way a sailor knows the stars can predict, just by looking, whether two substances will react, what they will form, and roughly how violently.

You only need to know a small handful of elements to follow most of chemistry that touches everyday life. Hydrogen, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen are the four pillars of biology. Sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium are the metals your body uses for signals and structure. Iron carries oxygen in your blood. Chlorine sits in your stomach acid. Phosphorus is in your bones and in the energy currency of every cell. Knowing where these few elements sit in the table, and which families they belong to, is enough to understand most of the chemistry of food, cooking, weather, and your own body.
