When two atoms stay together, what holds them? They do not have hands. They do not have ropes. The thing that binds them is called a chemical bond, and at root every chemical bond is a particular arrangement of electrons.

Recall that an atom has a nucleus surrounded by electrons in shells, and that the outermost shell is the one that matters for chemistry. Atoms feel most stable when their outermost shell is full. Helium and neon and argon, with their already-full outer shells, do not bond at all. They are content alone. Every other atom is, in some sense, looking for a way to fill or empty its outer shell. The various ways atoms accomplish this are the various kinds of chemical bond.

The first kind is the ionic bond. Imagine sodium meeting chlorine. Sodium has one lonely electron in its outer shell. Getting rid of it would leave sodium with a complete inner shell exposed, which is a stable configuration. Chlorine has seven electrons in its outer shell and would love to have an eighth. So sodium hands its outer electron over to chlorine. Now both atoms have full outer shells. But this electron transfer leaves sodium with one extra proton compared to its electrons, which means it carries a positive electric charge. Chlorine, having gained an electron, now carries a negative charge. The two ions, as charged atoms are called, are pulled together by the simple electric attraction between opposite charges. They stick. That stuck-together pair is sodium chloride, ordinary table salt. The bond between them is called ionic because it lives between ions.

You can almost see the picture by analogy with iron filings near a magnet. The filings line up because each one feels the magnetic pull. In a salt crystal, every sodium ion is pulled toward every nearby chloride ion, and every chloride is pulled toward every nearby sodium. The whole crystal is locked into a regular cubic pattern by these competing attractions. That is why salt crystals have those tidy little cubic shapes when you look at them under a magnifying glass.

The second kind of bond is the covalent bond. Imagine two hydrogen atoms approaching each other. Each one has a single electron and would like to have two, which would fill its first shell. Neither has anything to give, but they can share. They overlap their electron clouds and let the two electrons orbit both nuclei together. Each atom now sees two electrons in its outer shell most of the time. Both atoms are satisfied, and they are pulled to stay near each other so the sharing can continue. This is a covalent bond. The atoms have bonded by sharing electrons rather than transferring them.

Most molecules in your body are held together by covalent bonds. Water, H₂O, is two covalent bonds, one between each hydrogen and the central oxygen. Methane, CH₄, is four covalent bonds, one between each hydrogen and the central carbon. Sugars and fats and proteins and DNA are vast networks of covalent bonds. Covalent bonds are also why life chemistry is so rich. Sharing is more flexible than handing over. Atoms can share one pair, two pairs, or even three pairs, producing single, double, and triple bonds with different strengths and different geometries.

The third kind is the metallic bond. Take a piece of copper and look at it as a chemist. Each copper atom has loosely held outer electrons. When many copper atoms come together, they all let their outer electrons go into a kind of shared pool. The atoms become positive ions floating in a sea of electrons that drift through the whole piece of metal. This explains why metals conduct electricity so well; the electrons are already free to move. It also explains why metals are shiny, malleable, and ductile. The atoms can slide past each other without breaking the bonds, because the sea of electrons keeps reforming around them. You can hammer copper into a sheet without shattering it because of this. Hammer a salt crystal and it breaks into bits, because the rigid ionic pattern cannot accommodate the shift.

There is one more kind of bond worth mentioning, weaker than the others but enormously important. The hydrogen bond. When a hydrogen atom is covalently bonded to a strongly electron-pulling atom like oxygen or nitrogen, the hydrogen ends up with a slight positive charge because its electron is being held away from it. That slightly positive hydrogen can be attracted to another nearby atom that has a slight negative charge, like the oxygen in another water molecule. The attraction is much weaker than an ionic or covalent bond, but it is still real. Hydrogen bonds are why water clings to itself, why ice floats, why the two strands of DNA hold together while still being able to come apart for copying, and why proteins fold into the precise shapes that let them do their work.

You can think of these four kinds of bond as occupying a spectrum. Ionic bonds are full electron transfers and the strongest of the holding forces. Covalent bonds are even sharing and equally strong but more flexible. Metallic bonds are communal pooling of many electrons. Hydrogen bonds are gentle, fleeting attractions that depend on weak partial charges. Almost every substance in the everyday world is held together by some mixture of these four ideas. Understanding what kind of bond holds a substance together is half of understanding how that substance behaves.
