Every chemical reaction is also a transaction in energy. When atoms break old bonds and form new ones, the energy stored in those bonds is rearranged along with the atoms. Sometimes energy is released into the surroundings. Sometimes energy is absorbed from the surroundings. To understand a reaction fully, you have to follow the energy as well as the atoms.

Hold a lit match in your hand and you can feel the warmth coming off it. The match is releasing energy into the air. Touch a piece of metal that has been left in the sun and you feel the warmth that has soaked into it. Hold an instant cold pack against a sore knee and the pack feels cold. That cold pack contains a chemical reaction that absorbs energy from its surroundings, including from your knee. These are the two faces of chemical energy. Some reactions warm their environment. Some cool it.

A reaction that releases energy is called exothermic. The Greek prefix exo- means out, and thermic refers to heat. An exothermic reaction sends heat outward into the surroundings. Burning fuel is exothermic. The combustion of methane gives methane plus oxygen going to carbon dioxide and water plus heat. The atoms have rearranged from a higher-energy configuration into a lower-energy configuration, and the difference flowed outward as heat. The match, the candle, the campfire, the gasoline engine, all are exothermic.

A reaction that absorbs energy is called endothermic. The prefix endo- means inward. An endothermic reaction pulls heat inward from its surroundings. Dissolving ammonium nitrate in water is endothermic; the dissolved mixture feels colder than the water and the powder did separately, because energy has moved from the water and the air into the new arrangement. Photosynthesis is endothermic; plants pull energy out of sunlight and use it to combine CO₂ and water into sugar, building a higher-energy molecule from lower-energy reactants.

Where does this energy come from or go to? It is stored in the chemical bonds. Every bond between atoms holds a particular amount of energy, set by the kinds of atoms involved and the kind of bond. To break a bond, you have to put in that much energy. To form a bond, you get that much energy back out. A reaction is a long accounting of bonds broken and bonds formed. If the new bonds together hold less energy than the old ones, the difference comes out as heat, and the reaction is exothermic. If the new bonds together hold more, the difference must come from somewhere, usually the surroundings, and the reaction is endothermic.

You can sometimes guess which way a reaction will go by counting bonds. The bonds in CO₂ and H₂O are very strong, so reactions that produce them often release a lot of energy. That is why combustion is so reliably warm. Conversely, the bonds in O₂ and N₂ are also very strong, which is why making those molecules from their parts releases a lot of energy and breaking them apart costs a lot.

Even an exothermic reaction usually needs a little energy to get going. The candle does not light itself. You have to bring a flame near it. The hydrocarbon-oxygen reaction sits behind a small barrier. The first few bonds have to break before the new ones can form, and breaking them costs energy. Once the reaction is underway, the energy released breaks more bonds and the cycle sustains. The barrier is called the activation energy, and the initial input that gets you over it is called the spark or the ignition or, in more biological language, the activation. Every chemical reaction has some activation energy, even fast ones. Catalysts are substances that lower the barrier without being consumed, letting reactions proceed at lower temperatures. Enzymes in your body are biological catalysts that allow your cells to run reactions at body temperature that would otherwise need a furnace.

You can also describe energy changes in a reaction with a single number, called the enthalpy change, written ΔH. A negative ΔH means the reaction releases energy; the reaction is exothermic. A positive ΔH means the reaction absorbs energy; the reaction is endothermic. Tabulated enthalpies of formation tell you how much energy is bound up in any common compound, and from them you can predict the enthalpy of almost any reaction by simple arithmetic. Subtract the energies of the reactants from the energies of the products and you have the answer. This kind of bookkeeping is the daily bread of chemists who design fuels, refrigerants, propellants, batteries, or biological reactions in the lab.

Heat is not the only kind of energy that flows in a reaction. Some reactions release energy as light, like a glowstick after you snap it. Some release electrical energy, like a battery in a flashlight. Some release sound, like the small crack of a popping balloon during a violent gas reaction. The chemistry textbook often treats heat as the default, but the underlying principle is energy in any form. Reactions move energy from where it was stored in old bonds to wherever the new arrangement and its surroundings end up.

So when you watch a candle burn, when you taste cold sherbet, when you cup your hands around a hand warmer in winter, you are watching the energy ledger of a chemical reaction in real time. The atoms have not changed. They have only chosen new partners. And whenever atoms change partners, energy quietly moves with them.
