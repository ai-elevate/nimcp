Strike a match and watch it burn. The wood blackens, smoke curls up, the air around it gets warm, and after a few seconds nothing is left but a small bent stick of charcoal. Something has happened. The wood is gone. Yet no atoms have vanished. They have only rearranged.

This is a chemical reaction. A chemical reaction is what we call any process where atoms break apart from old combinations and snap into new ones. The substances you start with are called the reactants. The substances you end up with are called the products. The atoms themselves are simply borrowed from the reactants and lent to the products. Nothing is created. Nothing is destroyed.

The match is a good starting example. The wood is mostly long molecules of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, the kinds of molecules called cellulose. The air is full of oxygen molecules, O₂. When the match is hot enough, the cellulose molecules break apart and their carbon and hydrogen atoms grab oxygen atoms from the air. The carbon atoms pair with oxygens to form CO₂, carbon dioxide gas, which floats away invisibly. The hydrogen atoms pair with oxygens to form H₂O, water vapor, which also floats away. The energy that was stored in the bonds of the cellulose is released as heat and light. The match is now ash and the air is slightly warmer and slightly more humid. The atoms have all gone somewhere; they have just changed partners.

You can write this kind of rearrangement using a small notation. The reactants go on the left, an arrow points to the right, and the products are listed on the right. So the burning of methane gas, the simplest possible burning, looks like this. CH₄ plus 2 O₂ goes to CO₂ plus 2 H₂O. Read it like a sentence. One methane molecule, plus two oxygen molecules, becomes one carbon dioxide molecule plus two water molecules. The numbers in front of the molecules are called coefficients, and they tell you how many of each kind take part.

Notice the central rule. Count the atoms on each side. On the left there is one carbon, four hydrogens (in the methane), and four oxygens (two from each O₂). On the right there is one carbon, two hydrogens times two waters which gives four hydrogens, and two oxygens from the CO₂ plus two from the waters which gives four oxygens. The atoms balance. They have to. The atoms that came in must come out. This is the rule of conservation of mass, and it is one of the strictest rules in all of chemistry. Every honest chemical equation must balance.

When you cook an egg, a different kind of reaction happens. The clear part of the egg is mostly water with proteins dissolved in it. The proteins are long chains folded into delicate shapes. When you heat the egg, those folds come undone, and the chains tangle together into a solid network. The clear part turns white and opaque. No atoms have left, but the molecules have changed their shape and their relationships. The egg cannot be uncooked. Once the proteins have rearranged into the new tangle, putting them back into their original folds is essentially impossible. Many reactions are like this. Easy to do, hard or impossible to undo.

When you drop an antacid tablet into water, fizz appears. The fizz is bubbles of carbon dioxide gas. The tablet contained a compound, often sodium bicarbonate or calcium carbonate, that releases CO₂ when it meets water and acid. NaHCO₃ plus an acid gives sodium salt plus water plus CO₂. The bubbles you see are the new molecules being born and escaping into the air. This is also what is happening when you mix baking soda with vinegar. The vinegar is the acid; the baking soda is the bicarbonate; the violent bubbling is the carbon dioxide rushing out. Two ordinary kitchen ingredients, one extraordinary reaction.

Reactions need a nudge to start. Methane and oxygen can sit next to each other for a thousand years and not do anything. The atoms are happy where they are. To start the reaction you have to give a little push, usually a spark or a flame. That initial push provides the energy needed to break a few of the original bonds. Once those bonds are broken, the atoms snap into their new arrangement, and that snapping releases more energy, which breaks more bonds, which makes more atoms rearrange, which releases more energy. The reaction sustains itself in a chain. This is why a single match starts a fire that lasts for hours, and why a single spark can ignite a tank of gasoline.

Some reactions release energy. Some absorb it. Burning a match warms the air; that energy comes from the reactants, which had more chemical energy than the products. The heat is the difference, set free into the surroundings. Other reactions, like baking soda with vinegar, can actually feel slightly cold to the touch, because they need to pull energy in from the surroundings to proceed. We will return to this asymmetry later. For now, hold onto the picture. A chemical reaction is a careful trade of atoms between molecules, with energy flowing in or out as the trade is made.
