A carbon chain by itself is rather dull. A long string of carbons with hydrogens attached, called a hydrocarbon, mostly just burns when you light it on fire. To get the rich variety of molecules that make up life and medicine and food and fragrance, you need to attach other little chemical pieces to the carbon scaffold. Those little pieces are called functional groups. Each one gives the molecule a particular personality.

Think of a carbon chain as a skeleton and the functional groups as organs. Different organs hung on the same skeleton produce dramatically different creatures. The carbon backbone determines the size and shape; the functional groups determine the chemistry.

The first functional group worth knowing is the hydroxyl group, written -OH, an oxygen with a hydrogen attached. When you stick a hydroxyl onto the end of a carbon chain you get an alcohol. The simplest is methanol, one carbon with an OH. The next is ethanol, two carbons with an OH, which is the alcohol you find in beer, wine, and spirits. Add more carbons and you get propanol, butanol, and so on. Even though they all share that -OH, their behaviors differ. Methanol is poisonous; ethanol is intoxicating; the larger alcohols are increasingly oily. The hydroxyl group makes any of them more polar than the bare carbon chain would be, which is why ethanol mixes with water freely while gasoline does not.

Sugars are decorated with hydroxyls all over their carbon rings. That is why sugars dissolve in water so easily and why they taste sweet. The dense decoration of -OH groups makes them very polar and very interactive with water and with the receptors on your tongue.

The second group is the carbonyl, a carbon with a double bond to oxygen, written C=O. When the carbonyl carbon is at the end of a chain, the molecule is called an aldehyde. When it is in the middle of a chain, the molecule is called a ketone. The smell of vanilla comes from a molecule called vanillin, which has an aldehyde group. The smell of fresh cinnamon comes from cinnamaldehyde, also an aldehyde. The acetone in nail polish remover is the simplest ketone. Many of the warm baking smells in a kitchen are aldehydes and ketones drifting up from heated sugars and proteins.

The third group is the carboxyl, written -COOH. It is essentially a carbonyl with a hydroxyl attached to the same carbon, all bunched together. A carbon chain ending in a carboxyl group is called a carboxylic acid, and these molecules really are acids in the sense from earlier. Vinegar's sourness comes from acetic acid, a two-carbon chain with a carboxyl on the end. Citric acid in citrus fruit has three carboxyl groups, which is why lemons taste so sharply sour. Long fatty acids in butter and olive oil have a single carboxyl on the end of a much longer carbon chain. The carboxyl group is acidic; the chain length controls how oily or how water-friendly the rest of the molecule feels.

The fourth group is the amine, written -NH₂. Amines are basic in the sense that they can grab a proton and turn into -NH₃⁺. The smell of decaying fish comes from small amines escaping into the air. Most of the molecules called neurotransmitters, the chemical signals between nerve cells, contain amine groups. Adrenaline, dopamine, serotonin, and histamine are all built around amine functional groups. Take any amino acid, and you find one carboxyl group and one amine group on the same small molecule. That is what makes them amino acids. Strung together, they form proteins.

The fifth group is the ester, written R-COO-R, where R stands for any carbon chain. An ester is what you get when a carboxylic acid and an alcohol meet and lose a water. Esters have famously fruity and floral smells. The smell of pineapple, banana, pear, strawberry, jasmine, all come from esters. Perfumers and food chemists have a vast catalog of esters, each one matching a different fruit or flower. Many of them can also be made in the lab, which is why artificial banana flavor smells so strikingly like banana even though it is just a single small ester molecule.

The sixth group is the ether, written R-O-R. An ether is two carbon chains connected by an oxygen. Diethyl ether was the famous early surgical anesthetic, hence the name. Ethers are generally inert and pleasant smelling. Many fragrances and many of the components of fuel additives are ethers.

There are more, of course. Phosphate groups, written -PO₄, appear in DNA, RNA, and the energy currency molecule ATP. Sulfhydryl groups, written -SH, appear in some amino acids and stitch proteins together with disulfide bridges. Each functional group brings its own behavior and its own contribution to a molecule's overall personality.

Once you know the common functional groups, you can read a complicated molecule the way a literate person reads a sentence. You see a carbon chain. You see one alcohol here, two amines there, a carboxyl on the end. You can guess at how the molecule will dissolve, how it will smell, whether it is acidic or basic, what its shape might be. Modern chemistry is built on this kind of reading. The carbon scaffold gives the molecule a body. The functional groups give it a face.
