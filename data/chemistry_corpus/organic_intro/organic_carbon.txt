Of all the elements in the periodic table, one stands apart for the role it plays in life. That element is carbon. Almost every molecule in your body has a carbon backbone. The wood in a tree, the flesh of a fish, the petals of a rose, the soft pulp inside a strawberry, all built around carbon. Why carbon? What is so special about element number six?

The answer is in carbon's outer shell. A carbon atom has four electrons in its outer shell, exactly half of what a full shell holds. That perfect halfway position is the key. Carbon is not eager to lose all four electrons, because that would take a lot of energy. Carbon is not eager to gain four either. Instead, carbon shares. It forms covalent bonds, four of them, with whatever other atoms it meets. That means every carbon atom can act as a junction point with four neighbors.

Carbon is also content to bond with itself. A carbon atom can bond to another carbon atom, and that one to a third, and that one to a fourth, and so on, forming chains as long as you like. The chains can be straight or branched. They can fold back to form rings. They can fork into networks. No other element has this combination of strong, four-fold, self-bondable behavior at ordinary temperatures. Silicon, which sits below carbon in the periodic table and also has four outer electrons, comes close but cannot manage the same flexibility. So nature has built almost all of life on carbon.

Start with the simplest possible carbon-only molecule. Methane, CH₄. One carbon at the center with four hydrogens attached, one to each of the four bonds. Methane is a gas. It is the main ingredient in natural gas, the fuel that burns in many kitchen stoves. Now connect two carbons together and fill the remaining bonds with hydrogens. You get ethane, C₂H₆, also a gas. Three carbons in a row gives propane, C₃H₈, used in barbecue grills. Four gives butane, C₄H₁₀, used in cigarette lighters. The chains keep growing. Around five to seventeen carbons, the substance becomes a liquid at room temperature. That is the realm of gasoline and diesel and kerosene. Past seventeen or so, it becomes a thick wax, like paraffin. Past thirty, it becomes solid asphalt. From cooking gas to road tar, all the same chemistry, just with chains of different lengths.

Now consider what happens when you let the chains form rings. Six carbons can bond into a hexagonal ring with extra hydrogens attached, giving a molecule called benzene. The benzene ring is one of nature's favorite shapes. It shows up in coffee aroma, in cinnamon, in vanilla, in many medicines, in dyes, in plastics. Once you know to look for it, the hexagon shows up everywhere. Many of the smells you find pleasant come from molecules built around one or two benzene rings.

Adding other atoms to a carbon chain changes its behavior dramatically. A long carbon chain ending in an oxygen-hydrogen group, written -OH, becomes an alcohol. The simplest alcohol is methanol, one carbon plus an OH group. The next is ethanol, two carbons plus an OH group, which is the alcohol in beer and wine. Propanol, butanol, and so on follow. The carbon chain provides the body, and the OH group provides the personality. Take the same carbon chain and replace the -OH with a -COOH group, called a carboxyl group, and you get a fatty acid. The fatty acids in olive oil, butter, and your own body fat are all just carbon chains of different lengths with a carboxyl tip.

The richness of life comes from this combinatorial flexibility. Take a carbon chain and decorate it with various other small groups, called functional groups, and you get an enormous variety of molecules with different properties. Glucose, the sugar your cells run on, is a six-carbon ring decorated with five OH groups and one CH₂OH branch. Cholesterol, in your blood, is four fused rings of carbon decorated with one OH and a hydrocarbon tail. Caffeine is two fused carbon-nitrogen rings with three small extra groups attached. Each is just a particular pattern stamped onto a carbon scaffold.

The molecules of life are mostly long carbon chains and rings stitched together with smaller carbon-based pieces. Proteins are long chains of small carbon-based units called amino acids, all linked end to end. DNA is a long chain of larger carbon-based units called nucleotides. Fats are mostly long chains of carbons. Carbohydrates are linked carbon rings. The four big classes of biological molecules are all carbon constructions.

Because of carbon's geometry, organic molecules also have shapes. The four bonds around a carbon atom point outward toward the corners of an imaginary tetrahedron. This means that even a small molecule has a definite three-dimensional structure. Some molecules can come in left-handed and right-handed versions, mirror images that cannot be superimposed on each other, like your hands. Many biological systems care intensely about which version is which. Almost all the amino acids in your body are left-handed. Almost all the sugars are right-handed. Pharmaceutical companies must control handedness very carefully, because the wrong-handed version of a drug can be ineffective or harmful.

So the special chemistry of life is, at its core, the chemistry of carbon. One element, four-fold bonding, willing to chain to itself, willing to host an army of decorating groups. From that single chemical fact emerges the entire molecular machinery of biology.
