Pour water from a cup, watch steam rise from a kettle, hold an ice cube on your palm. Three different substances? No, just one substance, water, in three different forms. Solid, liquid, and gas. These are the three states of matter you meet every day.

Look closely at the ice cube. It has a fixed shape. You can carry it from the freezer to the table and it stays itself. The molecules inside the cube are locked into a rigid pattern. Each water molecule has a little spot in the lattice and stays there, jostling slightly but never wandering off. The molecules are still moving, every molecule is always moving, but in a solid the motion is small, like a child wiggling in a chair, not running around the room.

Now hold the cube in your hand. The warmth of your skin slips into the ice. The molecules wiggle harder. Eventually they wiggle so hard that the spots in the lattice cannot hold them anymore, and the lattice collapses. The molecules slide free. The cube has become a puddle. This is melting. The puddle is a liquid. In a liquid the molecules are still close together, almost as close as they were in the solid, but they are no longer in fixed positions. They roll over each other like marbles in a jar. That is why a liquid takes the shape of whatever container holds it. The molecules can move; they just stay near each other.

Heat the puddle on the stove. The molecules wiggle harder still. Some of them, near the surface, wiggle so hard that they break free of the others entirely and fly off into the air above. Steam. As more energy goes in, more and more of them break free, and finally the puddle is gone and the air above is full of fast-moving water molecules far apart from each other. That is a gas. In a gas the molecules are nearly independent. They fly around at hundreds of meters per second, bumping into each other and into the walls of any container, but otherwise free.

The pattern is simple. As you put energy into a substance, its molecules move faster. Slow molecules huddle together in fixed positions and form a solid. Faster molecules can slide past each other but stay near and form a liquid. Faster still and they fly apart and form a gas. The states of matter are stages of motion.

Each transition has a name. Solid to liquid is melting. Liquid to gas is boiling or, more gently, evaporation. Gas to liquid is condensation, which is what happens when warm humid air touches a cold drink and beads form on the outside of the glass. Liquid to solid is freezing. Solid to gas, skipping the liquid stage entirely, is sublimation. A block of dry ice, which is solid carbon dioxide, sublimes directly into a fog of CO₂ gas without ever puddling.

Each substance has its own personality of transitions. Water freezes at zero degrees on the Celsius scale and boils at one hundred. Iron, by contrast, has to be heated to over fifteen hundred degrees before it melts. Nitrogen, which makes up most of the air, has to be cooled all the way to nearly two hundred below zero before it becomes a liquid. Why so different? Because the molecules of different substances pull on each other with different strengths. Iron atoms hug each other tightly, so a lot of motion is needed to shake them loose. Nitrogen molecules barely cling at all, so they are flying freely as a gas at any temperature you live in.

Most substances expand when they melt and contract when they freeze. Water is famously stubborn about this. It expands when it freezes. That is why an ice cube floats and why a forgotten bottle of water in the freezer can crack. The molecules in solid ice arrange themselves in a roomy crystalline pattern with empty spaces between them, so the solid is less dense than the liquid. This odd behavior is rare among substances, and it has enormous consequences. It is why lakes freeze from the top down rather than the bottom up, which lets fish survive winter under the ice instead of getting trapped in a solid block.

A few substances exist in a fourth state, called plasma, which is a gas so hot that the electrons have been ripped off the atoms. The sun is a ball of plasma. So is a lightning bolt and the glowing inside of a fluorescent bulb. You can think of plasma as a gas with the protons and electrons no longer held together. Plasma plays almost no role in your kitchen, but most of the visible matter in the universe is in this state.

The takeaway is that states of matter are not different substances. They are different arrangements and different speeds of the same molecules. Heat is not a thing that gets added; heat is just the name we give to the increased motion of the molecules. Cool the same gas down enough, and it sinks into a liquid and then a solid. Heat the same solid up enough, and it climbs into a liquid and then a gas. Matter is one continuous game played at three or four different tempos.
