Water deserves a lesson of its own. It is the most familiar substance on the planet, and it breaks more rules than almost any other. Most of biology depends on the rules water breaks.

Look at a single water molecule, H₂O. Two hydrogen atoms attached to one oxygen atom. The atoms do not arrange themselves in a straight line. The two hydrogens come off the oxygen at an angle, like ears on a mouse. Because oxygen pulls electrons more strongly than hydrogen does, the oxygen end of the molecule carries a slight negative charge and the hydrogen end carries a slight positive charge. The molecule is lopsided, electrically. Such a molecule is called polar. Water is one of the most strongly polar small molecules in chemistry.

Polarity is the secret to almost everything water does. The slight negative on one molecule's oxygen end is attracted to the slight positive on another molecule's hydrogen end. They snap together gently. This particular kind of weak attraction is so important it gets a special name, the hydrogen bond. Liquid water is a vast crowd of molecules holding hands by hydrogen bonds, constantly breaking and reforming the connections as they jostle around.

The hydrogen bonds are why water is liquid at room temperature. Most molecules of similar size are gases. Methane, ammonia, hydrogen sulfide are all small molecules and all are gases unless cooled severely. Water alone, of comparable size, is a liquid. It clings to itself. The hydrogen bonds also raise its boiling point dramatically. If water did not have hydrogen bonds, it would boil somewhere around minus eighty degrees Celsius, and the planet would be dry.

Hydrogen bonds also explain why ice floats. When water freezes, the molecules lock into a rigid lattice with each one perfectly arranged to hydrogen-bond with four neighbors. That arrangement requires a lot of empty space. So solid ice is actually less dense than liquid water. An ice cube floats. Forgotten bottles in the freezer crack. Lakes ice over from the top down, leaving liquid water beneath where fish can live through the winter. If ice sank, lakes would freeze solid from the bottom up, the planet's biology would be confined to the tropics, and history would be very different.

Water dissolves more substances than any other common liquid. Drop a pinch of salt into a glass and it disappears. The salt, sodium chloride, breaks apart into separate sodium ions, each carrying a positive charge, and chloride ions, each carrying a negative charge. The polar water molecules swarm them. The negative oxygen ends point at the sodium ions, the positive hydrogen ends point at the chloride ions, and the salt is escorted away into solution. Sugar dissolves the same way, surrounded by water molecules grabbing at every polar group on its surface. Almost any compound with electrical lopsidedness can be dissolved by water. This is why blood, sap, sea water, and the inside of every living cell are all watery solutions teeming with dissolved chemistry.

But water cannot dissolve everything. Drop oil into water and the two stay apart. The oil floats on top in a slick. Oil molecules are not polar. They do not have lopsided charges to grip onto. Water molecules, busy hydrogen-bonding to each other, refuse to make room for the oil. The oil cannot push its way in, so it gets squeezed out. This same principle is why your salad dressing separates and why washing a greasy plate with cold water alone does not work. You need soap, which has one polar end and one oily end, to bridge the two worlds.

Water has a huge heat capacity. It takes a lot of energy to warm a given mass of water by even one degree, far more than for almost any other substance. This is again because of the hydrogen bonds. Some of the heat energy you put in goes into breaking hydrogen bonds rather than into making molecules move faster. The result is that the planet's oceans absorb enormous amounts of solar energy without dramatic temperature swings, smoothing out the climate. The same property is why your body, which is mostly water, can sit in a hot kitchen without your internal temperature spiking.

When water evaporates, it carries away a great deal of energy with it. That is why sweat cools you down. The fastest-moving water molecules at the surface of your skin break free and fly off, leaving behind the slower ones. The average energy of what is left is lower, which means the temperature is lower. The same principle keeps pots from boiling dry instantly, and it shapes weather across continents.

Almost everything about life on this planet is shaped by water's strange polite stubbornness. The cell is built around the rule that polar things mix with water and oily things hide from it. Membranes, proteins, DNA, the way energy moves through living tissue, all of it is choreographed by these few facts about a small bent molecule with two hydrogens and one oxygen. When you drink a glass of water, you are drinking the most chemically interesting substance you will meet today.
