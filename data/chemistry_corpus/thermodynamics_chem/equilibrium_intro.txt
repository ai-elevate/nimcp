Most chemical reactions you meet in a textbook are written with an arrow pointing from reactants to products. That picture is fine for a first pass, but it hides something important. Many reactions can run backward as well as forward. Reactants become products, and at the same time, products become reactants. The arrows point both ways. The system settles into a balance.

Imagine a closed glass jar, half full of water. Above the water there is air, and at first the air is dry. Water molecules at the surface, the fastest-moving ones, fly up into the air as vapor. As more water vapor builds up in the air, some of those vapor molecules slow down and bump back into the liquid surface and rejoin the water. Two opposing processes are now running. Water is leaving the liquid; water is entering the liquid. Eventually the rate at which water leaves equals the rate at which water enters. From outside, nothing seems to be happening. The water level in the jar is steady. The amount of vapor in the air is steady. But individual molecules are still flying back and forth as actively as ever. We say the system has reached equilibrium.

Equilibrium does not mean nothing is happening. It means the forward and backward rates have become equal, so the net result is unchanged. It is dynamic, not static. The image to keep in mind is two children on a playground, one filling a bucket from a stream while the other dumps the bucket back into the stream at the same speed. The bucket level holds steady, but neither child is idle.

The same idea applies to chemical reactions. Take some hydrogen gas and some iodine gas, seal them in a tube, and let them react to form hydrogen iodide. The reaction can be written H₂ plus I₂ goes to 2 HI. But HI molecules can also break apart into H₂ and I₂ again. So the arrows go both ways, written H₂ plus I₂ in equilibrium with 2 HI. After enough time, the mixture settles into a stable ratio of all three, with each species being made and unmade at exactly the same rate. The ratio depends on the temperature and pressure but not on which side you started from. If you start with all H₂ and I₂, the system slides forward to make HI. If you start with all HI, the system slides backward to make H₂ and I₂. Both starting points end up at the same balance.

The position of equilibrium depends on the conditions. Heat the mixture and the balance shifts. Compress it and the balance shifts again. There is a useful rule of thumb that captures these shifts, called Le Chatelier's principle. If you push on a system at equilibrium, the system shifts in the direction that pushes back. Add more H₂ and the equilibrium shifts toward making more HI to use up the extra. Take some HI away and the equilibrium shifts forward to make more. Compress the gas and the equilibrium shifts toward whichever side has fewer molecules in total, since that side takes up less space and relieves the pressure. The system always tries to undo the disturbance.

You can see this principle in everyday life. The blood in your veins carries oxygen using a protein called hemoglobin. In your lungs, where oxygen is plentiful, hemoglobin grabs onto oxygen and forms oxyhemoglobin. In your tissues, where oxygen has been used up, the equilibrium shifts the other way and hemoglobin releases oxygen. The chemistry is the same; only the local concentration changes, and Le Chatelier's principle does the rest. Without this responsiveness, no animal could live.

Equilibrium also explains why some reactions seem to stop short of completion. You might mix two reactants expecting them to combine fully and get only a partial conversion. The reaction is not lazy; it has reached equilibrium. The forward and backward rates have equalized somewhere in the middle. To get more product, you have to disturb the balance. Remove the product as it forms, so the equilibrium shifts forward. Add more of one reactant to push the system. Change the temperature in the right direction. These are the standard tricks of industrial chemistry. Almost every large-scale chemical process is the art of nudging an equilibrium where you want it.

The famous Haber process for making ammonia is exactly this. N₂ plus 3 H₂ in equilibrium with 2 NH₃. At ordinary temperature and pressure the equilibrium lies far to the left and very little ammonia forms. To shift it to the right, you raise the pressure, which favors the side with fewer total gas molecules. You also adjust the temperature carefully, hot enough to make the reaction go fast but not so hot that it shifts back the wrong way. You feed the unreacted nitrogen and hydrogen back into the reactor. By managing the equilibrium, you can pull billions of kilograms of ammonia out of the air every year, which is the basis of all modern fertilizer.

Equilibrium is one of the deeper ideas in chemistry. A reaction is not always a one-way trip. It is often a tug of war, with both sides pulling at once, and the position of the rope at any moment depends on the conditions. Once you see reactions as tugs of war rather than simple journeys, a lot of chemistry that seemed mysterious starts to make sense.
