Inflation and recession.

Inflation is a slow, broad rise in the prices of most things. Not just oranges, or fuel, or rent, but the general level. A loaf of bread that cost a dollar last year costs a dollar and three cents this year. A bus ride that cost two dollars now costs two dollars and ten cents. The numbers on every price tag drift upward together. Looked at the other way, each dollar buys a little less than it did before. Inflation is a slow erosion of what your money is worth.

A small amount of inflation is normal and even useful. Most central banks aim for around two percent a year. At that pace, prices roughly double every thirty-five years, which is slow enough that families can plan around it. Wages typically rise along with prices, so most people do not feel poorer in real terms, even though every individual price tag is rising. Mild inflation also makes recessions slightly less painful, because employers can let real wages drift down without having to actually cut anyone's paycheck, which is a thing workers find easier to accept than direct cuts.

Inflation becomes harmful when it goes much higher or becomes unstable. At ten percent a year, prices are doubling every seven years, which is fast enough that long-term contracts are hard to write and savings sitting in bank accounts visibly lose value. At fifty percent a year or worse, the situation becomes alarming. Workers cannot keep up. Anyone holding money is being robbed quietly. Trust in the currency erodes. In the most extreme cases, called hyperinflation, prices double in days or hours, and the entire monetary system collapses into barter or foreign currency. This has happened in real countries within living memory, and the human cost has been enormous.

Inflation has many possible causes, and arguments among economists about which cause matters most fill libraries. Two stand out. The first is too much money chasing too few goods. If the central bank prints a great deal of new money or the government runs huge deficits financed by money creation, while the actual production of goods does not rise at the same pace, the price level rises to absorb the extra money. The second is supply shocks. When a war disrupts oil supplies, or a pandemic disrupts global shipping, or a drought ruins a year's wheat harvest, the available supply of important goods falls sharply, and the prices of those goods leap upward, dragging the wider price level with them.

A recession is the opposite kind of trouble. Where inflation is too much demand pushing prices up, a recession is too little demand failing to keep production going. People stop buying as much, businesses respond by producing less and laying off workers, the laid-off workers buy even less, and the cycle feeds on itself for a while. GDP shrinks for two consecutive quarters or more. Unemployment rises. The mood of the country darkens. The pain is unevenly spread. Workers in the most exposed industries can lose jobs and homes within months, while those in stable jobs notice mostly that the news is grim.

Recessions can be triggered by a financial crisis, a sudden burst of pessimism, an external shock, or a deliberate slowdown by the central bank trying to cool an overheated economy. They can be brief and shallow or long and deep. The biggest recessions in modern history have left scars that took a generation to fully heal. Smaller recessions roll through every several years and are more or less normal punctuation in the longer growth story.

Inflation and recession are sometimes treated as opposites, the two failure modes between which the central bank tries to steer. In ordinary times, the trade-off looks roughly like a seesaw. Push the economy hard with low rates and stimulus, and you get fast growth and rising prices. Pull back with high rates, and you cool prices but risk pushing the economy into contraction. The central bank's hardest moments come when both troubles arrive at once, a situation called stagflation, where prices keep rising even as output and employment shrink. The standard tools point in opposite directions, and there is no easy answer.

For an ordinary household, the lessons are practical. During inflation, money in a savings jar is losing value, and money in a long-term productive investment is more likely to keep up. During a recession, having an emergency fund is the difference between weathering the storm and being capsized by it. Diversification across different kinds of assets, jobs in resilient industries, and habits of moderate spending are the personal-scale defenses against macroeconomic weather you cannot control. You will not change inflation or stop a recession. You can position yourself so that, when either arrives, you are not the most exposed person in the neighborhood.
