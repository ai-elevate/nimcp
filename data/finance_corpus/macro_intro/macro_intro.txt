An introduction to macroeconomics.

Most of what we have talked about so far has been microeconomics, which is the study of how individual people and individual businesses make decisions about money. Macroeconomics is the view from much higher up. It is the study of an entire country's economy considered as one large system. The total amount of stuff being made, the total number of people working, the average level of prices, the way the government and central bank steer the whole machinery. The microeconomic questions are about you and the corner store. The macroeconomic questions are about the whole country at once.

The single most important macroeconomic measurement is gross domestic product, or GDP. Imagine you could add up the value of every good and every service produced in a country over a year. Every loaf of bread, every haircut, every car, every legal consultation, every piece of software written. The total is the GDP. It is the country's annual output, expressed as a single number. When GDP grows, the country is producing more this year than last. When GDP shrinks, the country is producing less. Most of what people argue about in economic policy is, in one way or another, an argument about how to make GDP grow steadily without breaking other things in the process.

GDP is not the same as wealth. A country with high GDP one year and low GDP the next is not necessarily losing its houses and roads. The buildings are still there. What changes is the flow of new production. GDP is the river, not the lake. It tells you how much new value is being created in a given period, not how much already exists.

GDP also is not the same as well-being. A country can have rising GDP while ordinary people struggle, if the new wealth flows mostly to a small group. A country can have steady GDP while life genuinely improves, if work hours fall and the same output is produced more pleasantly. Economists know this. They watch GDP because it is one of the cleanest numbers available, but they treat it as a starting point, not a verdict.

The next big number is the unemployment rate. This is the percentage of people who are looking for work and cannot find any. A low unemployment rate, around three or four percent, usually means the economy is healthy and almost everyone who wants work has work. A high rate, eight or ten percent or more, means a lot of people who want to contribute are not contributing, which is a personal hardship for them and a productive loss for the country. Unemployment, GDP, and prices all move together in characteristic patterns, and reading those patterns is most of what economic forecasters do.

Above all of this sits a peculiar institution called the central bank. In your country it might be called the Federal Reserve, or the Bank of England, or the European Central Bank, or some similar name. The central bank does not run businesses or set the price of bread. What it controls is the cost of money itself, by setting a key short-term interest rate that ripples out into all other rates in the economy. When the central bank raises rates, borrowing becomes more expensive, people borrow less, businesses invest less, the economy slows, and inflation eases. When the central bank lowers rates, borrowing becomes cheaper, more activity gets started, the economy speeds up, and inflation can build. The central bank is steering a giant ship by adjusting one wheel.

Governments also affect the macroeconomy through what is called fiscal policy. They tax some activities and subsidize others. They spend money on roads, schools, defense, retirement payments, and a hundred other things. They borrow when they spend more than they collect. The shape of those choices, the level of taxes, the level of spending, the size of the resulting deficit or surplus, sets a kind of background condition that all the smaller decisions in the economy take place against.

So the macroeconomic picture has these large pieces. Total output, measured by GDP. Total employment, measured by the unemployment rate. The price level, measured by inflation. The cost of money, set by the central bank. The pattern of taxes and spending, set by the government. They all interact, none can be moved without affecting the others, and the dance among them is what fills the news every day.

When you read about a country's economy, you are usually reading about one of these levers being pulled or one of these numbers being released. The story can feel chaotic at first, but underneath, the cast of characters is small. Once you can name them, the pattern becomes legible.
