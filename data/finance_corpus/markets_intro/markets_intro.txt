An introduction to markets.

A market is just a place where people who want to sell meet people who want to buy. The earliest markets were literal. A square in a town, with farmers from the nearby countryside laying out vegetables on cloths, fishmongers calling prices, a baker at one end and a sandal maker at the other. People walked the square, looked at goods, asked prices, sometimes bargained, sometimes walked away. Out of all those small conversations, a kind of agreement emerged about what each thing was worth that day.

You can build a market in a classroom in five minutes. Give half the children apples and tell the other half they have small coins. Tell them they can trade or not, as they like. Watch what happens. Some apples will be sold for two coins. Some will be sold for three. Some will not be sold at all because the apple looks bruised or because the seller is asking too much. Within a few minutes, you will hear children negotiating, comparing, drifting toward a rough common price. They are doing the work of a market without anyone teaching them.

The two great forces in any market are supply and demand. Supply is how much of the thing is available to sell. Demand is how much people want to buy. When supply is low and demand is high, prices rise, because buyers compete with each other for the scarce supply, and they outbid each other to win it. When supply is high and demand is low, prices fall, because sellers compete with each other to attract the few buyers, and they undercut each other to win the sale. The balance point, where the amount people want to sell at the price equals the amount people want to buy at the price, is called the market clearing price. At that price, every seller who wants to sell finds a buyer, and every buyer who wants to buy finds a seller, and there is nothing left over.

A familiar example. The corner store gets a small batch of fresh bread every morning. If the price is set very low, by lunchtime there is no bread left and customers in the afternoon walk away disappointed. The price was too low. If the price is set very high, by closing time half the loaves are still on the shelf, and tomorrow they will be stale and worth nothing. The price was too high. The store owner experiments, day by day, until she finds a price where the last loaf is sold near the end of the day. That price clears the market. It is not arbitrary. It is the answer to a small daily question about how much bread, at what price, is what people in this neighborhood actually want.

Markets do something remarkable. They aggregate information that no single person has. The store owner does not know how hungry every customer is or how many other places they could buy bread. The customers do not know what flour costs the baker or how many loaves were baked. But out of many small decisions to buy or not, to lower the price or hold it firm, a single number emerges that summarizes the whole situation. The price tells the baker to bake more or less tomorrow. It tells customers that bread is plentiful or scarce. The price is, in this sense, a message that everyone reads and responds to.

This is why markets are so widely used to organize the production and distribution of things. They are not perfect. They can be manipulated. They can fail when buyers and sellers do not have honest information, or when one side has overwhelming power, or when the thing being traded is something that cannot really be owned, like clean air. We will return to those failures in a later lesson. But the basic mechanism is honest and efficient. Many small decisions, taken freely by people who know their own situation best, produce a coordinated outcome that no single person could have planned.

The market is a kind of distributed brain. Each buyer and seller is a small node, holding a tiny piece of the whole picture. The price is the signal that travels between them. When the signal is allowed to flow honestly, the result is bread on the shelf, fruit at the stand, books in the shop, all without any central authority deciding how many of each thing should be made and where they should go. When you understand this, a great deal of what looks chaotic about economics suddenly becomes orderly.
