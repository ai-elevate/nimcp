What money is, and why people invented it.

Imagine you have a bag of marbles and your friend has a sheet of stickers. You like stickers. Your friend likes marbles. So you trade. Three marbles for two stickers, and both of you walk away happy. This is the oldest kind of exchange in the world. It is called barter, and for most of human history it is how people swapped what they had for what they wanted.

But barter has a problem, and you can feel the problem the moment you try it. Suppose you want a sticker but your friend already has more marbles than she wants. She wants a piece of fruit. You don't have fruit. So you have to find someone with fruit who wants marbles, trade marbles for fruit, then bring the fruit to your friend, then trade the fruit for the sticker. Three trades to do the work of one. Most of the time, you don't even find that chain of people. The trade simply does not happen, and a thing that could have made everyone better off never occurs.

Money was invented to solve this problem. Money is anything that everyone in your community has agreed to accept, even if they don't personally want to use it for anything. If everyone accepts small shells, then you can trade marbles for shells, and shells for the sticker, and the chain works. The shells are not really the point. The point is the agreement. The shells are just a token that carries the agreement around.

Money has three jobs. It is a medium of exchange, which is the job we just described. It is a unit of account, which means it gives us a single ruler we can use to measure the worth of very different things. Without money, you would have to memorize how many marbles equal a sticker, how many stickers equal an apple, how many apples equal a bus ride. That is hundreds of separate numbers. With money, every one of those things has a price tag in the same unit, and you only have to remember one number per item. And money is a store of value, which means you can hold onto it and spend it later. You cannot easily save your wealth as fish, because fish go bad. You can save it as money.

For money to do these three jobs, it has to behave well. It has to be hard to fake, or counterfeiters would print it freely and make the existing money worthless. It has to be reasonably stable in value, or no one would trust it for saving. It has to be easy to carry, divisible into small amounts for small purchases, and durable enough to survive being passed from hand to hand.

Different cultures have used different things as money. Cattle, salt, copper rings, glass beads, silver coins, gold coins, paper notes, and now numbers in a computer. Modern money is mostly digital. When your parent pays for groceries with a card, no physical thing changes hands. A number in one bank's database goes down, a number in another bank's database goes up, and a receipt is printed. The whole transaction is information.

This is the strangest and most important thing about money. It is not really a thing. It is a shared belief. A dollar is worth a dollar because everyone in the country has agreed to act as if it is, and because there is a government and a banking system that work hard to keep that belief stable. If everyone suddenly stopped believing, the dollar would be worth nothing, even though the paper and the database entries would be exactly the same as before. This has happened in history, in countries where the trust collapsed. It is rare, but it is real, and it is why people who run central banks treat their job with great seriousness.

When you handle money, then, you are handling a piece of social trust. You are using a tool that lets you trade with strangers you will never meet, store the result of work you did last year, and plan for things you want to do next year. That is a remarkable invention, and almost no one notices it, because it works so well that it has become invisible.
