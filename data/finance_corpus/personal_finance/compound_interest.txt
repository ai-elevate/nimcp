Compound interest, and why time is on your side.

There is an old puzzle. Would you rather have one million dollars right now, or one penny today, doubling every day for thirty days? Many people choose the million without thinking. The right answer is the penny. After thirty days of doubling, a single penny becomes more than five million dollars. The trick is not in the penny. The trick is in the doubling, and the doubling is what compounding looks like when it has a long enough runway to take off.

Simple interest is the kind a child first learns. You lend a friend ten dollars, and at the end of the year she pays you eleven. The extra dollar is the interest, and the rate is ten percent. If she keeps the loan for two years and you charge simple interest, she owes twelve dollars. Each year adds the same dollar.

Compound interest is different. Each year, the interest itself starts earning interest. After year one, you have eleven dollars. The second year's ten percent is calculated on the eleven, not on the original ten, so the second year adds a dollar and ten cents instead of a flat dollar. After year two, you have twelve dollars and ten cents. After year three, the ten percent is calculated on twelve dollars and ten cents, and so on. The growth keeps accelerating, because the base keeps growing.

In the early years, the difference between simple and compound is small and looks unimportant. After ten years, ten dollars at ten percent simple becomes twenty dollars, while at ten percent compound it becomes about twenty-six. A small gap. After thirty years, the simple total is forty, and the compound total is one hundred and seventy-four. A four-fold gap. After fifty years, simple is sixty, and compound is over a thousand. The gap is a chasm.

This is the central insight that makes long-term saving so powerful. The dollar you save at age twenty has fifty years to compound before retirement. The same dollar saved at age fifty has fifteen years. The early dollar is worth several times the late dollar, even though they look identical the day you put them in. People who start early need to save much less to end up with much more, simply because their money has more years to compound.

There is a quick estimate that financial people use, called the rule of seventy-two. Divide seventy-two by the annual interest rate, and you get the rough number of years it takes for an investment to double. At seven percent a year, money doubles every ten years or so. At ten percent, every seven years. At three percent, every twenty four years. The rule is not exact, but it is accurate enough for back-of-envelope thinking, and it gives you a feel for how much rate matters. Doubling every ten years instead of every twenty turns one doubling in a working career into three or four. The compounding does the work that you, by yourself, never could.

The same machinery runs in reverse, and it is just as powerful. If you carry a credit card balance at twenty percent a year, and you only pay the minimum, the unpaid interest joins the balance and starts earning more interest. A few hundred dollars left to compound on the wrong side can grow into thousands within a few years. The compounding does not know which side you are on. It simply multiplies. People who borrow at high rates and do not pay quickly are giving compound interest a chance to work against them, and it does so with the same patience that builds wealth in the other direction.

The lesson is simple, and worth carrying with you. Time is the most valuable input in any financial plan. You cannot manufacture more of it, and you cannot recover the years that have already passed. The dollar you save this year, in a sensible long-term investment, may be worth ten or twenty times that much by the time you need it. The dollar you owe this year, at a high interest rate, may be worth ten or twenty times that much by the time you finally pay it off. Treating time as a resource, and starting early on the right side of the equation, is one of the few choices in personal finance whose effects truly compound.

This is also why the advice you will hear from anyone who knows the subject is some version of the same thing. Start saving early, even if the amount is small. Avoid expensive debt, even when the borrowing feels small. Be patient with the slow boring growth, because the slow boring growth, given enough time, becomes the most powerful force in your financial life.
