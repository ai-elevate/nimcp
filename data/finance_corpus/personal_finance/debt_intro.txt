Debt, credit, and the price of trust.

Suppose your friend asks to borrow your favorite book. You hand it over. A week passes, then a month, and the book is still on her shelf. You ask for it back, and she promises tomorrow, and tomorrow does not come. Eventually you stop asking. The next time she asks to borrow something, you hesitate. You may say no. The friendship has a small new wound that did not exist before.

This is the simplest model of debt. To borrow is to make a promise to return. Debt is a promise about the future, made now. The promise has a price, because the lender is giving up something useful for a stretch of time, and they want to be sure they will get it back, and they want a small reward for the inconvenience and the risk.

When you borrow money, the price of the promise is called interest. If you borrow one hundred dollars and agree to repay one hundred and ten dollars next year, the extra ten dollars is interest. It compensates the lender for waiting and for the chance that you will not pay them back at all. The yearly rate, ten dollars on a hundred, is ten percent. Interest rates can be much higher than this, especially on credit cards, where the rates can climb above twenty or thirty percent a year. Rates can also be very low, especially on government bonds and mortgages, where the lender feels confident the loan will be repaid.

Credit is the willingness of others to lend to you. Some debts are reasonable. A house is so expensive that almost no one buys one with cash, and a sensible mortgage at a low rate, paid back over many years, is how most families come to own a home. A loan to learn a skill that increases your earnings can pay for itself many times over. A loan to start a business that succeeds turns a small amount of borrowed money into a much larger amount of new wealth.

Other debts are dangerous. Borrowing at twenty percent interest to buy something you will throw away by next year is one of the worst trades a person can make. The thing wears out. The debt does not. You keep paying for it long after you have forgotten what you bought. Many people fall into this trap because the borrowing is easy and the bill comes later, when the pleasure is gone and the regret has nowhere to go.

There is a useful test. Before you borrow, ask whether the thing you are buying will still be valuable when the loan is paid off. A house, yes. An education that opens new doors, often yes. A meal at a restaurant, no. A pair of shoes, no. The first kind of borrowing is sometimes called good debt, and the second kind is sometimes called bad debt. The labels are imperfect, but the test underneath them is sound.

Debt also creates a record. Banks and credit reporting agencies keep track of every loan you have taken and every payment you have made. This record is your credit history. If you pay your debts on time, the record gives you access to better loans at lower interest rates in the future, which compounds into a quieter financial life. If you pay late, or fail to pay, the record makes future borrowing expensive or impossible, and the higher rates you pay can keep you trapped in difficulty for years. A first job, a first apartment, sometimes even a first phone, may all depend on this record. So your handling of small debts now becomes the foundation for what you can do later.

The deepest lesson of debt is the lesson your friend taught you when she did not return the book. Trust is the underlying currency. When you borrow and repay, you spend a little trust and earn a lot of it back. When you borrow and do not repay, you destroy trust that was hard to build. Lenders, employers, landlords, future business partners, all of them are going to ask, in their own way, can this person be relied on. The answer is written, day by day, in the way you handle the promises you have already made.
