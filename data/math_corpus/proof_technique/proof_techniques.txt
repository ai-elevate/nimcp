Proof techniques: induction, contradiction, contrapositive, construction.

A proof is a sequence of statements, each justified by a previous statement, an axiom, or a definition, ending with the claim to be established. The five most common proof techniques each suit a different shape of claim.

Direct proof. To show that P implies Q, assume P, then chain definitions, axioms, and prior theorems until you reach Q. Most basic facts are proved directly. Example: if n is even then n-squared is even. Assume n is even, so n equals 2k for some integer k. Then n-squared equals (2k)-squared equals 4k-squared equals 2 times (2k-squared). Since 2k-squared is an integer, n-squared is twice an integer, so n-squared is even.

Proof by contrapositive. To show that P implies Q, instead show that not-Q implies not-P. The two are logically equivalent. Useful when the negation is easier to work with than the original. Example: if n-squared is even then n is even. The contrapositive: if n is not even then n-squared is not even. Assume n is odd, so n equals 2k plus 1. Then n-squared equals 4k-squared plus 4k plus 1, equals 2 times (2k-squared plus 2k) plus 1, which is odd. So n-squared odd whenever n odd, which is the contrapositive of the original claim.

Proof by contradiction. To show P, assume not-P, then derive a contradiction — some statement that is both true and false in the same argument. Since not-P leads to absurdity, not-P must be false, so P must be true. The classical example: there is no largest prime. Suppose, for contradiction, there were one. Then the list of primes is finite. But Euclid's construction (multiply them all and add one) produces an integer not divisible by any of them, hence having a prime factor not in the list. Contradiction. So there is no largest prime.

Proof by contradiction is also how we prove that the square root of two is irrational. Assume it is rational; then it equals p over q in lowest terms, p and q with no common factor. Squaring: p-squared equals 2 q-squared. So p-squared is even, so p is even (by the contrapositive proof above). Write p as 2r. Then 4 r-squared equals 2 q-squared, so q-squared equals 2 r-squared. So q-squared is even, so q is even. Now both p and q are even — they share a factor of two. But we assumed they had no common factor. Contradiction. So the square root of two is not rational.

Proof by induction. To show that P(n) holds for every positive integer n, prove two things. Base case: P(1) is true. Inductive step: assume P(k) is true for an arbitrary positive integer k, then prove P(k plus 1). Together these establish P(n) for all n by a chain of implications: P(1) gives P(2) gives P(3) and so on indefinitely. Example: the sum of the first n positive integers equals n times (n plus 1), all over two. Base case: sum to 1 is just 1, and 1 times 2 over 2 is also 1. Inductive step: assume the formula holds at k. Then sum to (k plus 1) equals (sum to k) plus (k plus 1), equals k(k plus 1)/2 plus (k plus 1), equals (k plus 1)(k plus 2)/2 by factoring. That matches the formula at (k plus 1), so induction completes the proof.

Proof by construction. To prove that something exists, exhibit it. To show there is an even number greater than three, write down four. To show that a number can be written as a sum of two squares, give the squares. Some constructive proofs are routine; others require considerable ingenuity. Construction is the technique of choice when the claim is "there exists" — the existential quantifier wants a witness.

Each technique fits a shape of claim. Direct proof and contrapositive both establish implications; the choice between them is which side is easier to start from. Contradiction handles negative claims (no such object exists) cleanly. Induction handles claims about all positive integers. Construction handles claims about existence. Most real proofs combine techniques — induction whose inductive step uses contradiction, contrapositive whose conclusion was first established by construction.

Beyond these five there are diagonalization (Cantor's argument that the reals are uncountable), pigeonhole (placing more objects than slots forces some slot to contain two), well-ordering (every nonempty subset of the natural numbers has a smallest element, which makes induction possible), and many others. The vocabulary of proof technique is one of the most refined products of human reasoning.
