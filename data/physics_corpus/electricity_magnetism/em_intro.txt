Charge, current, and the electromagnetic field.

Rub a balloon vigorously on a wool sweater, then hold the balloon near small bits of paper. The paper jumps up to the balloon. Hold the balloon near your hair, and your hair lifts to meet it. Something has been transferred from the sweater to the balloon, and that something now reaches across empty space to pull on objects nearby. The phenomenon is electricity, and the something transferred is electric charge.

There are two kinds of charge, and we call them positive and negative. Two objects with the same kind of charge repel each other; two objects with opposite charges attract. The names are a historical accident — Benjamin Franklin chose them more than two centuries ago, when no one knew what charge actually was. Today we know that ordinary matter is made of atoms, that each atom has a positive nucleus surrounded by negative electrons, and that what was transferred onto your balloon was a small excess of electrons rubbed off the wool. Charge is the count of these tiny tickets, positive minus negative, and it never appears or vanishes — only moves from one place to another.

Like gravity, the electric force is one of the great forces of nature. Two charges separated by some distance pull or push on each other with a strength proportional to the product of the two charges, divided by the square of the distance between them. Double the distance, the force drops to a quarter. Triple the distance, the force drops to a ninth. This is the same inverse-square law that gravity obeys, and that fact alone hints that the two forces are deeply related in their structure, even though they are not the same.

Electric charge can sit still, as on the rubbed balloon, and it can move. When charge moves, we have an electric current. A current is a flow of charge — usually electrons — through some material, and we measure it by how much charge passes a point per second. The wire from your wall outlet to your lamp carries a current; the lamp glows because energy is being delivered along the wire and dissipated in the filament as light and heat.

To make charges move, you need an electric field. An electric field is a region of space in which a charge feels a force. Hold a positive test charge near a negatively charged object, and you feel a pull toward it; that pull is the electric field at that location. The field has direction (the way the force on a positive charge would point) and strength (how hard the force pulls per unit of charge). Wherever there are charges, there are fields. Wherever there are fields, there are forces on any charges that wander into the region.

Now magnetism. Hold a small iron nail near a refrigerator magnet, and the nail leaps toward it. Pour iron filings around a bar magnet on a sheet of paper, and the filings arrange themselves into elegant curving lines that loop from one end of the magnet to the other. Those lines are revealing the magnetic field, the magnetic counterpart of the electric field. Any place where a compass needle is deflected, there is a magnetic field.

For most of human history, electricity and magnetism looked like two separate things. Then, in the early nineteenth century, Hans Christian Ørsted in Denmark made a startling observation: a compass placed near a wire carrying an electric current was deflected by the wire. The current was creating a magnetic field. Almost simultaneously, Michael Faraday in England made the converse discovery: a moving magnet near a wire causes a current to flow in the wire. Changing magnetism creates electricity; moving electricity creates magnetism. The two fields are linked.

A few decades later, James Clerk Maxwell wrote down a small set of equations summarizing all the rules for electric and magnetic fields. The equations had a remarkable property: they predicted that an oscillating electric field would generate an oscillating magnetic field, which would in turn generate another oscillating electric field, and so on. The pattern would propagate through space as a wave. Maxwell calculated the speed of that wave from his equations and found, to his astonishment, that it matched exactly the known speed of light. Light, he concluded, was an electromagnetic wave. With that single connection, the fields of optics, electricity, and magnetism were unified.

Today we use this unification constantly. The current in a power line creates a magnetic field; that field, when changing, induces a current in another nearby line — this is how a transformer works. A spinning magnet inside a coil of wire generates a current — this is how a generator works. A current passing through a coil creates a magnet, which can be made to push and pull on a permanent magnet — this is how an electric motor works. Antennas radiate electromagnetic waves; antennas receive them; signals carry voices and pictures across the world. All of this is one phenomenon, with two faces.

Charge, current, electric field, magnetic field — these four ideas, woven together by Maxwell's equations, account for almost everything you see, hear, or touch in modern life. The chemistry of your body, the light from the sun, the radio in your kitchen, the lightning in a summer storm — all are forms of electromagnetism.
