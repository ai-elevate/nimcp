Fields and the way forces reach across space.

When you push a book across a table, the force you exert reaches the book through direct contact. Your hand touches the book; the book moves. This is intuitive, and Newton's mechanics handles it well. But how does the Earth pull a falling stone, when nothing visible connects them? How does a magnet attract iron filings without touching them? How does the Sun, ninety million miles away, hold the planets in their orbits? The answer that physics gives is the idea of a field.

A field is a property of space itself. At every point in space, you imagine a number — or, more usefully, a vector with magnitude and direction — that tells you what would happen to a small test object placed at that point. The Earth fills the surrounding space with a gravitational field; everywhere near the Earth, that field points toward the Earth's center, and an object placed there feels a pull along the field. A magnet fills space with a magnetic field, with a particular pattern of strength and direction at each point. A charged object fills space with an electric field. Wherever a field is present, anything sensitive to that field — another mass, another magnet, another charge — feels a force.

This sounds, at first, like a renaming of the original puzzle: we were already saying the Earth pulls the stone, and now we are saying the Earth's field pulls the stone, which is just one extra word. The reason fields matter is that they are real, in the sense that they carry energy and information independently of the object that produced them. Take the Sun away suddenly — it does not actually do this, but suppose it did — and the Earth would not feel the change instantly. The gravitational field has to propagate outward from where the Sun was, at the speed of light. For about eight minutes, the Earth would continue to orbit a Sun that was no longer there. The field is not just a bookkeeping device; it has its own life.

The same is true of the electric and magnetic fields. Wave a magnet, and the disturbance in the magnetic field travels outward at the speed of light. That traveling disturbance can be received by a distant antenna and turned back into a current — this is what radio is. Information has been carried, by the field, from one place to another, with no material substance moving between them. The field is the carrier.

A useful image: pin a sheet of paper to a corkboard and arrange iron filings on it. Now hold a bar magnet underneath the paper. The filings spring into curving lines that loop from one end of the magnet to the other. You are seeing the field. Each filing, slightly magnetized by its neighbors, lines itself up along the local direction of the magnetic force, and the resulting pattern shows you which way the field points everywhere on the page. Strong field, the filings cluster densely; weak field, they spread out. The bar magnet itself does not directly grab any individual filing — the field does, at each filing's location.

Electric fields look very similar around a charged object. Around a single positive charge, the field points outward in all directions, like the spokes of a wheel. Around a negative charge, the field points inward. Around two opposite charges close together, the field forms a graceful pattern of curving lines, leaving the positive charge, looping around, and entering the negative one. These pictures are not just diagrams; they are real depictions of the energy-carrying structure surrounding the charges.

Force, in the field picture, is what happens when an object enters a field. A positive charge in a positive-pointing electric field is pushed in the direction of the field. A negative charge is pushed against the field. A current in a magnetic field is pushed sideways, perpendicular to both the current and the field — this is the force that drives every electric motor. A mass in a gravitational field is pulled along the field. The field tells the object how to move; the object, by its own presence, modifies the field for everyone else.

This last point is critical. Fields are not produced once and left to sit there. Every charge produces an electric field around itself. When that charge moves, the field changes. Two charges therefore continually adjust each other's fields, and the resulting motion is the integrated effect of those continuous adjustments. In a quiet, steady situation, you can simplify and treat the fields as fixed; in a rapidly changing one, the fields swirl and propagate, and the calculation gets harder. But the principle is unchanged: each object responds to the field at its location, and contributes to the field everywhere else.

By the time you understand fields, you have moved a long way from Newton's picture of forces pushing and pulling at a distance. In the modern picture, there is no action at a distance — only objects, and the fields they create, and the fields acting on objects in turn. This way of thinking turned out to be the right one. The same field idea, generalized, became the foundation for all of twentieth-century physics, from quantum mechanics to general relativity.
