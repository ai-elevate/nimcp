Work, kinetic energy, potential energy.

Energy is one of the strangest and most useful ideas in physics. You cannot point at it; it has no color, no smell, no shape. You only know it by what it does: it makes things move, or warms them up, or lifts them, or lights them. And it is conserved — the total amount of it in a closed system never changes, only shifts from one form to another. This conservation is what makes the idea so powerful.

Begin with a simpler word: work. In everyday speech, work is anything that takes effort. In physics, work has a precise meaning. Work is done when a force moves an object through a distance along the direction of the force. Push a box across a floor, and you are doing work on the box. Lift a bag of groceries onto a counter, and you are doing work on the bag. Hold the bag motionless above the floor, and you are not doing physics work at all, however tired your arm may get — there is no motion, so no work in the technical sense.

The amount of work equals the force multiplied by the distance moved in the force's direction. Push with twice the force, do twice the work. Push the same way over twice the distance, do twice the work. Push sideways on something while it moves forward — perpendicular to your push — and you do no work, because the motion is not along your force.

Now imagine throwing a ball. While your hand is moving forward and pushing the ball, you are doing work on the ball. The ball is gaining speed. Whatever it is the ball gains from your work, we will call energy. Specifically, energy of motion, or kinetic energy. The faster something moves, and the more massive it is, the more kinetic energy it has. The exact form is one-half times mass times velocity squared, but for now the picture matters more than the formula: a slow car has a little kinetic energy; a fast car has a lot; a parked car has none at all.

Why do we say the work goes into the ball as kinetic energy? Because if the ball later strikes something and slows back down, the same amount of energy comes back out — as a thump, a dent, a sound, perhaps some warmth. Work in, energy stored, energy released. The accounting balances.

Now lift a book onto a high shelf. While you are lifting, you are doing work against gravity — pushing the book upward through a distance. Once the book is on the shelf, where has the work gone? It is no longer kinetic energy: the book is sitting still. We say the work has been stored as potential energy. The book has the potential to fall, and if it does, it will gain kinetic energy as it falls. Push it off the shelf, and the potential energy converts back into kinetic energy. By the time it reaches the floor, all the energy that you put in by lifting it is moving with the book — and then turns into a thump and a small amount of heat as the book hits.

Gravitational potential energy depends on mass and height. Lift twice the mass to the same height, store twice the energy. Lift the same mass to twice the height, store twice the energy. Near the Earth's surface, the potential energy is mass times gravity times height — the height referenced from whatever level you choose to call the floor.

Other kinds of potential energy exist too. A stretched spring stores potential energy in the bonds of its coiled metal; release it, and the energy goes into motion. A drawn bow stores energy in the bent wood; release the string, and the arrow flies. A battery stores energy in its chemicals; close the circuit, and the energy emerges as light or heat or motion. In every case, the same accounting works. Energy is put in, energy is stored, energy is released — and the totals match.

Watch a child on a swing. At the highest point of the arc, the swing is momentarily at rest. All the energy is potential — the child is high above the lowest point, with no motion. As the swing falls, height drops, and motion grows. At the bottom of the arc, the swing is moving fastest. All the energy is now kinetic — the child is at the lowest point, moving most quickly. Then up the other side, motion shrinks, height grows. At the far high point, motion is gone again, all the energy is back in potential. The trade is perfect, except for the small losses to friction and air resistance, which slowly drain the swing.

This trade-off — kinetic into potential and back — runs throughout physics. It runs in falling stones, in vibrating strings, in pendulums, in planets orbiting the sun. The story is always the same: a fixed total of energy, redistributed among forms, never destroyed, never created. Once you see the world this way, what looked like a chaos of unrelated motions starts to look like a careful bookkeeping in which nothing is lost.
