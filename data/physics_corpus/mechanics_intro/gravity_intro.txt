Gravity, weight, and free fall.

Drop a stone, and it falls. This is the most ordinary thing in the world, and also one of the deepest. Why does the stone fall? Why does it fall down rather than sideways? Why does it fall faster the longer you let it fall? These are physics questions, and the answers will lead you all the way out to the planets and back.

The Earth pulls everything near it toward its center. We call this pull gravity. Gravity is a force, like the push of your hand on a door, except that it acts at a distance — the Earth does not need to touch the stone in order to pull it. Every object with mass attracts every other object with mass; the Earth simply happens to be the largest mass close at hand, and so its pull dominates everything we do.

When you stand still on the ground, gravity is pulling you downward. The ground pushes you upward with an equal and opposite force, and the two cancel; you do not accelerate, you stand. If the ground vanished — say, you walked off the edge of a cliff — the upward push would vanish too, and only gravity would remain. With a net downward force, you would accelerate downward. You would fall.

Galileo, working in Italy four hundred years ago, made a startling discovery: in the absence of air resistance, every object near the surface of the Earth falls with exactly the same acceleration, regardless of its mass. A feather and a hammer, dropped together in a vacuum, hit the ground at the same instant. This sounds wrong; experience tells us heavy things fall faster than light things. But experience here is misleading. What slows a falling feather is the air it has to push out of the way. Take the air away, and the feather drops as fast as the hammer.

The acceleration of a freely falling object near the Earth's surface is about 9.8 meters per second per second, often written as g. Every second, a falling object's downward velocity grows by another 9.8 meters per second. After one second of falling, it is moving 9.8 meters per second; after two seconds, 19.6; after three seconds, 29.4. The growth is steady because the gravitational force is steady.

Why doesn't a heavier object fall faster? At first this seems to violate Newton's second law: more force should mean more acceleration. The trick is that a heavier object also has more mass, and the two effects cancel exactly. Gravity pulls on a stone with a force proportional to the stone's mass; the stone's mass also resists acceleration in the same proportion. Double the mass, and you double both the pull and the resistance. The acceleration comes out the same.

Weight and mass are not the same thing, even though we often confuse them in everyday speech. Mass is how much matter is in an object, and it does not change wherever you go. Weight is the gravitational force on the object, and it depends on where you are. On the surface of the Moon, where gravity is about one-sixth as strong, your weight would be one-sixth what it is on Earth; your mass would be unchanged. An astronaut floating in orbit has the same mass she had on the launch pad, but her apparent weight is zero, because she is in free fall along with her spacecraft.

Newton's deepest insight about gravity was that the force pulling an apple to the ground and the force holding the Moon in orbit around the Earth are the same force. The Moon does not fall straight down because it is also moving sideways, fast enough that as it falls it keeps missing the Earth. Its sideways motion and its falling motion combine to make a curve — an orbit. The same is true of the Earth around the Sun, the planets around the Sun, and any satellite around any planet. Orbital motion is just falling, indefinitely, at the right sideways speed.

Newton wrote down a law of universal gravitation: any two objects attract each other with a force proportional to the product of their masses, divided by the square of the distance between them. Double the distance, and the force is one-quarter as strong. Triple it, and the force is one-ninth. This inverse-square law accounts for the orbits of planets, the tides on Earth from the Moon, and the trajectory of any thrown ball. With one short formula, Newton tied the heavens to the Earth.

Three centuries later, Einstein gave a deeper account of gravity, in which mass curves the geometry of space and time itself, and objects in free fall follow the straightest possible paths through that curved geometry. We will come to that. For now, hold on to Newton's picture: gravity is a force between every pair of masses, it pulls inversely with the square of distance, and it gives every freely falling object the same acceleration, no matter what the object's mass.
