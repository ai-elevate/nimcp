Quanta, photons, and the strangeness of the very small.

Classical physics — the physics of Newton, of Maxwell, of nineteenth-century thermodynamics — assumed that everything in nature could vary smoothly. Energy could be any value at all. Light could have any color, any brightness, any combination of colors. Position and motion of any particle could be specified to any precision you cared to measure. By the start of the twentieth century, that picture began to crack. The cracks ran small at first and then widened, and out of them came quantum mechanics — a deep and thoroughly strange theory that has nonetheless turned out to be the most accurately tested physical theory in history.

The first crack came from the way hot objects glow. A red-hot iron, a yellow-hot poker, a blue-white star — different temperatures produce different colors of glow. Classical physics tried to predict the spectrum of light emitted by a glowing object and got an answer that was demonstrably wrong: it predicted infinite energy at very short wavelengths, an absurdity. Max Planck, in 1900, found the right formula by making a peculiar assumption: energy could not be emitted or absorbed in any continuous amount, but only in discrete chunks, which he called quanta. Each chunk had an energy proportional to the frequency of the light. With this strange assumption, the glow spectrum came out exactly right.

Five years later, Einstein extended the idea. Light itself, he proposed, comes in discrete chunks — particles of light, which we now call photons. A beam of light is a stream of these particles, each carrying an amount of energy equal to a constant, called Planck's constant, multiplied by the light's frequency. Red light has lower-frequency photons, each carrying less energy; ultraviolet light has higher-frequency photons, each carrying more.

This explained another puzzle, the photoelectric effect. Shine light on a metal, and electrons fly out — but only if the light's frequency is high enough. Below a certain threshold frequency, no electrons emerge no matter how bright the light. Above the threshold, electrons emerge, and they leave faster as the light's frequency rises, but their speed does not depend on brightness. Classical wave theory could not explain this. Photons could: a single photon below the threshold lacks enough energy to dislodge a single electron, and turning up the brightness only sends more such useless photons. A single photon above the threshold delivers enough energy in one go.

So light is sometimes a wave and sometimes a particle. The same is true, surprisingly, of matter. A few decades after Einstein's photons, Louis de Broglie proposed that ordinary particles — electrons, protons, atoms — also have wave properties, with a wavelength inversely proportional to their momentum. The idea was tested by firing electrons at a thin crystal and watching them produce a diffraction pattern, the unmistakable signature of waves. Electrons, undeniably particles, were also undeniably waves. So were neutrons, atoms, even small molecules.

This is the wave-particle duality of quantum mechanics. Every entity in the quantum world has both wave and particle aspects, and which aspect you see depends on what experiment you do. Pass a beam of electrons through two narrow slits, and you see a wave-like interference pattern on a screen behind the slits, just like water waves through two openings. Watch the electrons one at a time, and they arrive at the screen as discrete dots, each one a particle. Many particles, accumulated, build up the wave-like pattern. Each individual electron, in some sense, has gone through both slits at once.

Heisenberg's uncertainty principle puts a deep limit on what we can know about a quantum particle. The more precisely you measure a particle's position, the less precisely you can know its momentum, and vice versa. This is not a flaw of our instruments. It is built into the structure of quantum mechanics, and through Planck's constant it ties to the wave nature of matter. A wave with a sharp position has a broad spread of wavelengths, and therefore a broad spread of momenta; a wave with a sharp momentum has a single, definite wavelength, and therefore an indefinite position. You cannot have both at once, no matter how clever the measurement.

This means the world at the smallest scales is not deterministic in the old sense. You cannot say where an electron is and where it is going. You can only say what the probabilities are of finding it here or there, with this momentum or that. The probabilities are exact and predictable, given by a wave-like object called the wavefunction. But the outcomes of single measurements are not predictable, only their statistics over many measurements.

Einstein himself never fully accepted this. "God does not play dice," he famously said. But he was wrong, in this case. Decades of experiment have confirmed quantum probability with extraordinary precision, and the structure of the universe at the atomic scale is, as far as we can tell, irreducibly statistical.

Quantum mechanics is responsible for a great deal of modern life. Lasers, transistors, semiconductors, MRI scanners, atomic clocks, the entire chemistry of matter — all of these rest on quantum behavior. The world around you, however ordinary it looks, is built from quanta. The strangeness goes all the way down.
