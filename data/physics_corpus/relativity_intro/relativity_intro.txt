Frames of reference, the speed of light, and Einstein's relativity.

Imagine you are sitting in a train moving smoothly along a straight track. The shades are drawn, the ride is perfectly steady, and you cannot see outside. You toss a ball up in the air; it comes straight back down into your hand. You pour a cup of water; it falls neatly into the cup. As far as anything inside your compartment is concerned, you might as well be standing still. There is no experiment you can perform within the moving train that will tell you whether the train is moving or not — provided the motion is steady.

This observation, made informally by Galileo and made precise by Einstein, is the principle of relativity. The laws of physics look the same to anyone moving at a steady velocity. There is no special, privileged state of "really at rest." A passenger on the train and a watcher on the platform each have a perfectly valid perspective on the world. The train is moving in the watcher's view, and the platform is moving in the passenger's view. Both descriptions are correct.

Each observer has what we call a frame of reference: a system of coordinates and clocks against which she measures positions, velocities, and times. The watcher on the platform measures the train moving past her. The passenger measures her seat as stationary and the platform sliding by in the other direction. The same physical events look different in the two frames, but the underlying laws of motion are the same.

This is not new with Einstein. Galileo already knew it for ordinary motion. The strange and stunning addition Einstein made, in 1905, came from one extra fact: the speed of light is the same in every frame of reference.

Sit on a train moving fast and shine a flashlight forward. The light leaves the flashlight at one fixed speed — call it c, about three hundred million meters per second. Now ask the watcher on the platform to measure the speed of that same beam of light. By ordinary thinking, the platform-watcher should see the light moving at c plus the speed of the train: the light's speed in your frame, added to your speed in the watcher's frame. But that is not what is observed. The watcher measures the same speed c. The train's velocity does not add to the light's. The same holds in every frame: every observer, regardless of her own motion, measures light traveling at exactly c.

This sounds impossible. How can two observers in motion relative to each other both measure the same beam of light to be moving at the same speed? Something has to give. What gives, Einstein realized, is the assumption that time and space are the same for everyone.

Take a moving observer's clock and compare it carefully with a stationary observer's clock — and you will find they tick at different rates. The faster the relative motion, the larger the difference. A clock in steady motion runs slowly, as measured from a stationary frame. This is not a malfunction; it is built into the structure of space and time. The same is true of lengths: an object moving relative to you is shortened along its direction of motion. And events that are simultaneous in one frame are not simultaneous in another. These three effects — time dilation, length contraction, and the relativity of simultaneity — all conspire together to keep the speed of light the same for everyone.

The amounts involved are tiny at everyday speeds. A clock in a passenger jet runs slow compared to one on the ground, but the difference adds up to only about a billionth of a second over an hour-long flight. The effect is real, though, and has been measured directly by carrying atomic clocks aloft. At very high speeds — say, in the high-energy beams of a particle accelerator — the effects are huge. Particles that ought to decay in a tiny fraction of a second can be observed traveling considerable distances before they decay, because their internal clocks, from our point of view, are running slow.

A famous consequence is the equivalence of mass and energy. Einstein showed that mass and energy are two faces of the same thing, related by his most famous equation: E = m × c². Even a small mass corresponds to an enormous amount of energy, because c² is an enormous number. The energy released in a nuclear reaction comes precisely from a tiny conversion of mass into energy according to this rule. The fusion that powers the Sun follows the same accounting.

What we have just sketched is the special theory of relativity. It applies whenever motion is uniform — that is, no acceleration. A few years after special relativity, Einstein extended the picture to include gravity. The result, the general theory of relativity, says that mass and energy curve the geometry of space and time itself, and that what looks like a gravitational pull is actually objects following the straightest possible path through that curved geometry. A planet orbiting the Sun is not really being pulled in a curve; it is moving as straight as it can through a region of space-time that the Sun has curved.

Both theories have been tested with breathtaking precision. The GPS in your phone has to correct for both special and general relativity to give you accurate positions; without those corrections, the locations would drift by miles per day. The strange picture is the right picture.
