Heat, temperature, and the laws of thermodynamics.

Hold a metal spoon over a candle flame for a moment, then touch the handle. The handle, far from the flame, has grown warm. Something has traveled from the flame end of the spoon to the handle end. We call that something heat. Heat is a form of energy in transit between objects at different temperatures, flowing from the warmer to the cooler. Thermodynamics is the part of physics that deals with heat and the rules it obeys.

Begin with temperature. Temperature is a number that measures how hot something is, but underneath that simple description lies a deeper meaning. A solid object is made of countless atoms, all of them jiggling and vibrating in place. A liquid or gas is made of molecules in still freer motion, drifting and colliding. Temperature, at the microscopic level, is a measure of the average kinetic energy of all this jostling. A hot cup of tea is full of fast-moving molecules; a cold glass of water is full of slow ones. When you stick your finger in either one, your finger's molecules collide with the tea's or water's, exchanging energy with them, and you feel warmth or chill.

Heat flows naturally from hot to cold and never the other way on its own. Place a hot cup on a cool table, and the cup cools while the table warms. The cup never spontaneously becomes hotter while the table cools further. This direction of flow seems obvious, but it is one of the deepest facts of physics. The second law of thermodynamics, which we will reach soon, is fundamentally about why this asymmetry exists.

Heat is measured in the same units as any other energy. When we add heat to an object without changing its phase — that is, without melting or boiling it — the object's temperature rises. The exact relationship between heat added and temperature gained depends on the object. A given amount of heat raises the temperature of a small mass more than a large one, and raises the temperature of some materials more than others. Water, in particular, requires a great deal of heat to warm up by one degree, which is why coastal climates are mild — the surrounding ocean acts as an enormous heat reservoir, slow to warm in summer and slow to cool in winter.

The first law of thermodynamics is simply the conservation of energy applied to systems that include heat. The change in a system's internal energy equals the heat added to it minus the work done by it on its surroundings. If you put heat into a sealed gas, you raise its internal energy; if the gas pushes a piston outward, it does work on the piston and that work comes out of the energy balance. There is no free heat and no free work. Energy goes in, energy comes out, and the books always balance.

This first law immediately rules out a familiar fantasy. A perpetual motion machine of the first kind — a device that produces more energy than it consumes — must violate energy conservation. No such machine has ever worked, because no such machine can.

The second law of thermodynamics is more subtle. It says that in any spontaneous process, a quantity called entropy never decreases. Entropy is, roughly, a measure of disorder — of how many microscopic ways the atoms of a system can be arranged consistently with what you see at the large scale. A neat sandcastle has low entropy; the same sand strewn around the beach has high entropy. The wind disturbs the sandcastle and the sandcastle gradually becomes a flat patch of sand. The wind never collects flat sand into a sandcastle by itself. Disorder grows; order does not arise spontaneously.

This is the deeper reason heat flows from hot to cold. When a hot region and a cold region come into contact, energy spreads out. The hot region had its energy concentrated in fewer, faster molecules; the cold region had its energy spread among more, slower ones. Mixing the two distributes the energy more evenly and corresponds to many more microscopic arrangements. The system moves toward higher entropy and stays there. The reverse process — a uniformly warm region spontaneously dividing into a hot half and a cold half — is not forbidden, only astronomically unlikely. The improbability is so extreme that we treat it as impossible.

A consequence of the second law is that no engine can convert heat entirely into work. A car engine takes in heat from burning fuel, converts some of it into the motion of the car, and necessarily dumps the rest as exhaust heat to the environment. The efficiency of any heat engine is bounded by a number that depends only on the temperatures of the hot source and the cold sink. This bound was first calculated by Sadi Carnot in the early nineteenth century, before the atomic theory of heat was widely accepted, and it has stood ever since.

Thermodynamics also gives you the gas laws. The pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas are tied together by a clean relationship: pressure times volume is proportional to the number of molecules times the temperature. Squeeze a gas, and either its pressure rises or some of it escapes. Heat a gas in a sealed container, and the pressure rises. Cool it, and the pressure falls. These rules govern engines, weather, lungs, and stars.

The science is austere, but the picture it gives is breathtaking. Behind every warm object is a swarm of jostling atoms; behind every flow of heat is the spreading-out of energy among more configurations; behind every irreversible process is the relentless growth of entropy. The universe runs because it is settling.
