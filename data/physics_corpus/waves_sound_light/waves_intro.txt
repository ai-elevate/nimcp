Waves, sound, and the propagation of light.

Toss a pebble into a still pond. Rings of disturbance spread outward from the splash, growing wider, fainter, slower. A leaf floating on the surface bobs up and down as each ring passes, but the leaf itself does not travel outward — it returns, more or less, to where it started. Something has moved across the pond, and yet no water has gone anywhere. What is moving is a pattern, a shape in the surface — a wave.

This is the central idea of waves: they are organized disturbances that carry energy from place to place without permanently moving the medium they pass through. Sound passes through air; the air does not blow with it. Light passes through space; even space itself, in the modern understanding, can carry waves. To understand waves is to understand a great deal of physics.

A wave has a few characteristic numbers. Its wavelength is the distance between two repeating features — say, between one crest of a water wave and the next. Its frequency is how many of those features pass a fixed point each second. Its amplitude is how big the disturbance is — for water, how high the crests rise and how low the troughs fall. Its speed is how fast the pattern itself moves through space. Wavelength and frequency are tied together: their product is the speed. A long, slow ocean swell and a short, choppy harbor wave both move through the water, but the swell has a longer wavelength and a lower frequency.

Sound is a wave in air. When a guitar string vibrates, it pushes the air on one side, then pulls it back as it swings the other way. The push compresses the air slightly; the pull rarefies it. These compressions and rarefactions travel outward from the string at the speed of sound, about 343 meters per second in ordinary room air. Your eardrum receives the alternating compressions and rarefactions and translates them into nerve signals; your brain hears the note.

Pitch corresponds to frequency. A high frequency is a high pitch — the rapid push-and-pull of a small bird's song. A low frequency is a low pitch — the slow rumble of distant thunder. A child's voice typically reaches higher frequencies than an adult's because the child's vocal cords are shorter and vibrate faster. A double bass produces low frequencies because its strings are long and thick and vibrate slowly.

Loudness corresponds to amplitude. A whisper is a small pressure variation; a shout is a much larger one; a thunderclap is enormous. Push the air more, and the wave carries more energy. Two whispers do not add up to a shout, because amplitudes do not always add simply — but the qualitative picture is right. Bigger pushes, louder sounds.

Light is also a wave, but a stranger one. It is not a vibration of any material substance, but of the electric and magnetic fields that fill all of space. It travels through air, through glass, through water, and even through pure vacuum, where there is no medium at all. Its speed in vacuum is enormous — about 300 million meters per second, the fastest speed anything can move. We give that speed its own letter: c.

Different colors of light correspond to different frequencies, just as different pitches of sound do. Red light has a longer wavelength and lower frequency than blue light. Violet is the highest-frequency visible light; red is the lowest. Beyond violet lies the ultraviolet, invisible to your eyes but capable of burning your skin. Beyond red lies the infrared, the warmth you feel from a fire even before your skin touches it. Microwaves, radio waves, X-rays, gamma rays — all of these are light, of frequencies our eyes cannot register.

Waves can do a few characteristic tricks. They can reflect off surfaces — light bouncing from a mirror, sound echoing from a canyon wall. They can refract when they pass from one medium to another, bending because their speed changes; this is why a straw appears bent in a glass of water, and why a prism splits white light into a rainbow. They can diffract, bending around corners and through openings — which is why you can hear a voice through an open doorway even when you cannot see the speaker. They can interfere, two waves from different sources meeting in the same place and either reinforcing each other into a louder sum or canceling each other into silence. These four behaviors — reflection, refraction, diffraction, interference — are the unmistakable signatures of waves, and physicists rely on them to identify wave behavior wherever it appears.

Sound and light are very different in detail, but their wave structure is the same. Both have wavelength, frequency, amplitude, and speed, all related in the same way. Both can reflect, refract, diffract, and interfere. Once you grasp the wave idea, you have a framework that stretches from a pebble in a pond to the radio waves that carry voices across continents.
